## Part 1: Defining and Understanding Psychological Disorders

**Q: What is a psychological disorder?**
A: A collection of symptoms marked by a clinically significant disruption to a person's thoughts, emotions, or behaviors that causes distress or suffering. Thoughts, feelings, or behaviors are considered dysfunctional or maladaptive when they interfere with normal, day-to-day life — that interference, not just how unusual a behavior looks, is what separates a disorder from ordinary human variation.

**Q: How has our understanding of psychological disorders changed over time?**
A: Historically, "madness" was often blamed on demonic forces or moral failing, and treatments could be brutal. Around 1900, the discovery that syphilis could cause mental symptoms helped launch the **medical model** — the view that psychological disorders are like physical illnesses, with symptoms that can be diagnosed and treated, often in a hospital. Today's **biopsychosocial approach** goes further, viewing disorders as arising from the interaction of biological (genes, brain chemistry), psychological (stress, coping, learned thought patterns), and social-cultural (culture-specific stressors and norms) factors.

**Q: What is the vulnerability-stress (diathesis-stress) model?**
A: The idea that individual dispositions (vulnerabilities) combine with environmental stressors to trigger a psychological disorder. Epigenetics research supports this model: the same gene may be expressed in one environment and stay dormant in another.

**Q: What is comorbidity?**
A: The co-occurrence of two or more disorders in the same person (for example, major depressive disorder alongside an anxiety disorder), often reflecting overlapping genetic risk factors.

**Q: How and why do clinicians classify psychological disorders?**
A: The **DSM-5-TR** (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Text Revision) is the most widely used classification tool. Classification gives clinicians a shared vocabulary, helps predict a disorder's course, suggests treatment, and guides research. A newer complementary approach, the **Research Domain Criteria (RDoC)** project, organizes disorders by behavior and brain activity rather than symptom checklists alone.

**Q: What are the benefits and risks of diagnostic labels?**
A: Benefits: labels help professionals communicate and study causes/treatments, and can relieve clients by naming their suffering. Risks: labels can create stigma, bias perceptions (e.g., that people with disorders are dangerous), and — as the text notes — have historically been applied unequally across race and socioeconomic status.

## Part 2: Anxiety-Related Disorders

**Q: What are anxiety disorders, and how do generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, and specific phobias differ?**
A:
- **Generalized anxiety disorder** — continual, free-floating tension and worry about many things, for no obvious reason, lasting six months or more.
- **Panic disorder** — recurrent, unpredictable **panic attacks** (minutes-long episodes of intense dread and physical arousal) along with fear of the next attack; can lead to **agoraphobia**, the fear/avoidance of public places from which escape might be difficult.
- **Specific phobia** — persistent, irrational, excessive fear of a specific object or situation (heights, spiders, enclosed spaces, etc.).
- **Social anxiety disorder** — intense fear of judgment in social settings, leading to avoidance of social contact.

**Q: What is OCD, and how does it differ from everyday habits?**
A: **Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)** involves unwanted, intrusive, persistent thoughts (obsessions) and/or repetitive behaviors (compulsions) performed to reduce the anxiety those thoughts cause. Everyday rituals (checking that the door is locked once) cross into disorder only when they persistently interfere with daily functioning and cause distress (checking it ten times). Related disorders include hoarding disorder and body dysmorphic disorder.

**Q: What is PTSD?**
A: **Posttraumatic stress disorder** involves recurring vivid, distressing memories and nightmares, social withdrawal, jumpy anxiety, and trouble sleeping following exposure to trauma (combat, assault, disaster). Most people who experience trauma show resilience rather than developing PTSD; risk rises with the intensity of trauma-related distress.

**Q: What is a somatic symptom disorder?**
A: A disorder in which people experience genuinely felt physical symptoms with significant distress and impaired functioning, without an identifiable physical cause. **Illness anxiety disorder** (formerly hypochondriasis) involves interpreting normal bodily sensations as signs of serious disease, despite medical reassurance.

**Q: How do conditioning, cognition, and biology each contribute to anxiety-related disorders?**
A:
- **Conditioning** — fears can form through classical conditioning (an object paired with a scary event) and be maintained through operant reinforcement (avoidance reduces anxiety, so avoidance is repeated). **Stimulus generalization** can spread a fear from one event to similar events.
- **Cognition** — people can learn fears by observing others (e.g., wild-reared monkeys learning to fear snakes by watching other monkeys), and anxious people tend to be hypervigilant, interpreting ambiguous stimuli as threatening.
- **Biology** — genes influence fearfulness (twin studies), the brain's danger-detection system can become hyperactive, and natural selection may have prepared humans to fear ancestral threats (snakes, heights, storms) more readily than modern ones (guns, outlets).

## Part 3: Substance Use Disorders

**Q: What is a substance use disorder?**
A: A diagnosis given when continued use of a **psychoactive drug** (a chemical that alters perception, mood, and behavior) significantly disrupts a person's life. Key features include **tolerance** (needing more of the drug for the same effect) and **withdrawal** (unpleasant symptoms when use stops), along with diminished control, diminished social functioning, and hazardous use. Severity ranges from mild to severe depending on how many diagnostic indicators are present.

**Q: What are the three major categories of psychoactive drugs?**
A:
- **Depressants** (alcohol, barbiturates, opioids) — calm neural activity and slow body functions.
- **Stimulants** (caffeine, nicotine, cocaine, amphetamines, methamphetamine, Ecstasy) — excite neural activity and speed up body functions.
- **Hallucinogens** (LSD, psilocybin, marijuana in mild form) — distort perception and produce sensory images without external input.

**Q: What are the effects of alcohol?**
A: In any amount, alcohol is a depressant. Low doses can disinhibit behavior by slowing brain regions that control judgment; higher doses slow reactions, impair memory, and can be life-threatening (e.g., by suppressing the vomiting reflex during overdose). **Alcohol use disorder** involves tolerance, withdrawal, and continued use despite significant harm.

**Q: How do opioids and nicotine illustrate tolerance and withdrawal?**
A: Repeated opioid use causes the brain to reduce its own endorphin production, so when the drug is withdrawn, the person experiences painful withdrawal. Nicotine reaches the brain in about 7 seconds and is considered at least as addictive as heroin or cocaine; quitting produces cravings, insomnia, anxiety, and irritability.

**Q: What biological, psychological, and social-cultural factors influence substance use disorders?**
A:
- **Biological** — specific genes are linked to alcohol, nicotine, and cannabis use disorders, often via effects on the brain's dopamine reward system; twin and adoption studies show hereditary risk.
- **Psychological** — feelings of meaninglessness, depression, or trauma can make psychoactive drugs an appealing (if temporary) escape.
- **Social-cultural** — peer use, family attachment, and cultural/religious norms strongly predict adolescent substance use; misperceiving how much peers use or approve of drugs increases one's own use.

## Part 4: Depressive Disorders and Bipolar Disorders

**Q: What distinguishes major depressive disorder from ordinary sadness?**
A: **Major depressive disorder** is diagnosed when a person experiences at least five of nine specified symptoms (must include depressed mood or loss of interest) for two or more weeks, in the absence of substance use or another medical condition — symptoms such as changes in appetite/sleep, loss of energy, feelings of worthlessness, trouble concentrating, and thoughts of death. Ordinary sadness in reaction to a real loss is a normal, even adaptive, response; depression is a persistent disruption of functioning.

**Q: What are bipolar disorders?**
A: Disorders marked by periods of **mania** — a euphoric, talkative, highly energetic, overly ambitious state lasting a week or longer — that can alternate with periods of depression. **Bipolar I disorder** involves full manic episodes; **bipolar II disorder** involves depression alternating with a milder hypomania.

**Q: How do the biological and social-cognitive perspectives explain depression?**
A:
- **Biological** — depression and bipolar disorders run in families (heritability of major depressive disorder ≈ 40%); norepinephrine and serotonin are scarce during depression; brain reward-center activity decreases in depression and increases in bipolar mania.
- **Social-cognitive** — depressed people tend toward **rumination** (brooding, overthinking) and a self-blaming, stable, global **explanatory style** for negative events. **Learned helplessness** from uncontrollable bad events can also produce depressive hopelessness. A depressed mood can itself trigger more pessimistic thinking via **state-dependent memory**, creating a vicious cycle of stress → negative explanation → hopelessness → behaviors that invite more stress.

## Part 5: Schizophrenia

**Q: What are the symptoms of schizophrenia?**
A: Schizophrenia is a **psychotic disorder** — a split from reality — marked by:
- **Positive symptoms** (inappropriate behaviors present): **hallucinations** (false sensory experiences, often hearing voices), **delusions** (false beliefs, often paranoid), disorganized speech (**word salad**), and inappropriate emotional displays.
- **Negative symptoms** (appropriate behaviors absent): **flat affect**, toneless voice, motionless or rigid behavior, social withdrawal.

**Q: How do chronic and acute schizophrenia differ?**
A: **Chronic (process) schizophrenia** develops slowly, often after a long history of social difficulty, and carries a poorer prognosis with more negative symptoms. **Acute (reactive) schizophrenia** appears suddenly, often triggered by life stress in a previously well-functioning person, and has a better prognosis with more positive symptoms that respond to drug therapy.

**Q: What brain and prenatal factors are linked to schizophrenia?**
A: The **dopamine hypothesis** proposes that excess dopamine activity contributes to positive symptoms (supported by dopamine-blocking drugs reducing symptoms, and dopamine-boosting drugs worsening them). Brain scans show enlarged fluid-filled ventricles, thinner cortical tissue, and reduced frontal lobe activity in many people with schizophrenia. The **fetal-virus hypothesis** links prenatal viral infection (especially in the second trimester) to increased risk, supported by associations between schizophrenia and flu epidemics, dense-population birth areas, and winter/spring births.

**Q: What role do genes play in schizophrenia?**
A: Risk rises with genetic relatedness to someone with the disorder — from about 1 in 270 in the general population to roughly 1 in 10 with an affected sibling or parent, and close to 1 in 2 for an identical twin. Genome studies have identified hundreds of contributing genes, each with a small individual effect, consistent with a gene-environment interaction model.

## Part 6: Dissociative, Personality, and Eating Disorders

**Q: What is dissociative identity disorder (DID), and why is it controversial?**
A: **DID** involves two or more distinct identities that appear to control a person's behavior, typically emerging after overwhelming trauma. Skeptics point to its sharp historical rise in diagnosis rates and question whether it can be an extension of normal identity-shifting or a product of therapist suggestion; other researchers cite distinct brain and body states across identities as evidence it is a genuine, trauma-linked disorder.

**Q: What are the three clusters of personality disorders?**
A: The DSM-5-TR's ten personality disorders — enduring, inflexible patterns that impair social functioning — form three clusters:
1. **Anxious** cluster (e.g., avoidant personality disorder)
2. **Eccentric/odd** cluster (e.g., schizotypal personality disorder)
3. **Dramatic/impulsive** cluster (e.g., borderline, narcissistic, and **antisocial personality disorder**)

**Q: What biological and psychological factors are linked to antisocial personality disorder?**
A: People with antisocial personality disorder show a lack of conscience from a young age, along with impulsivity and little fear or remorse. Biological correlates include unusually low physiological arousal to stress, smaller/less-active amygdala, and reduced frontal lobe activity (impairing planning and impulse control). These biological vulnerabilities can combine with negative environments (abuse, family instability) to shape antisocial behavior.

**Q: What are the three main eating disorders?**
A:
- **Anorexia nervosa** — self-starvation and significant weight loss driven by a persistent fear of being fat, regardless of actual weight.
- **Bulimia nervosa** — cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors (vomiting, laxatives, fasting, excessive exercise); weight often stays within/above normal range.
- **Binge-eating disorder** — the most common eating disorder; significant bingeing followed by distress, without compensatory purging or excessive exercise.

**Q: What factors make some people more vulnerable to eating disorders?**
A: Heredity plays a substantial role (50–60% heritability estimates for anorexia). Family environments that are competitive, high-achieving, and protective, along with low body satisfaction, perfectionistic standards, and cultural pressure toward thinness (especially in food-abundant Western cultures), also contribute.

## Part 7: Risk of Harm to Self and Others

**Q: What factors increase suicide risk, and what do we know about nonsuicidal self-injury?**
A: Suicide risk rises with prior depression, feelings of hopelessness, a sense of being a burden or not belonging, and access to lethal means; risk can actually increase as severe depression begins to lift and energy returns. Individual acts remain very difficult to predict — most people with any single risk factor never attempt suicide. **Nonsuicidal self-injury (NSSI)** — deliberate self-harm without suicidal intent — is often self-reinforcing, providing relief from negative emotion, a way to signal distress, or a means of self-punishment; it is a risk factor for future suicidal thoughts but does not typically lead directly to suicide.

**Q: Does having a psychological disorder make someone dangerous?**
A: No. Research consistently finds that most people with psychological disorders are not violent, and most violent people do not have a diagnosed psychological disorder. Stronger predictors of violence include substance use, prior history of violence, and access to firearms. People with disorders are, in fact, more often victims than perpetrators of violence.

## Part 8: Neurodevelopmental Disorders

**Q: What is intellectual developmental disorder?**
A: A disorder apparent before age 18, diagnosed when a person has an intelligence test score around 70 or below **and** significant difficulty meeting independent-living demands in conceptual, social, and practical domains. It results from a combination of genetic and environmental factors.

**Q: What is autism spectrum disorder (ASD)?**
A: A neurodevelopmental disorder marked by social-communication deficits and repetitive behaviors, with a wide range of severity. Researchers debate whether an impaired theory of mind underlies the difficulty many autistic people have reading facial expressions and inferring others' mental states. Both genetic and prenatal environmental factors contribute; there is no evidence linking vaccines to ASD.

**Q: What is ADHD, and why is its diagnosis debated?**
A: **Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder** involves extreme inattention and/or hyperactivity-impulsivity that interferes with functioning. Because normal childhood energy, impulsivity, and distractibility exist on a continuum with ADHD symptoms, and because diagnostic criteria have broadened over time, some researchers and clinicians debate whether the condition is being over-diagnosed.
