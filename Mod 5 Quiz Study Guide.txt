Mod 5 Quiz # Chapter 9 Study Guide
## People, Plants, and Animals in the Past
### ANTH 103 · Kelly & Thomas, *Archaeology* 7th ed.

---

## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Chapter 9 is about how archaeologists reconstruct ancient diet, subsistence, and environments from the biological remains people left behind. Those remains fall into two broad categories: **faunal remains** (animal bones, studied by zooarchaeologists) and **plant remains** (studied by paleoethnobotanists). Neither kind of evidence speaks for itself — interpreting what it means requires the same middle-level theory introduced in Chapter 8: uniformitarianism, analogies, and taphonomy. The chapter walks through several interlocking methods — faunal analysis, palynology, phytolith analysis, lipid analysis, and wood rat midden studies — and two major case studies that show these methods doing real work: the Folsom-period Agate Basin site in Wyoming, and the Chavín de Huántar ceremonial center in Peru.

---

## Part 1: The Basics of Faunal Analysis

**Q: What is a faunal assemblage, and how does it differ from a paleontological assemblage?**
A: A faunal assemblage is the collection of animal bones recovered from an archaeological site where humans played some role in the accumulation. A paleontological assemblage, by contrast, contains animal remains that accumulated entirely through natural processes. The distinction matters because archaeological assemblages must be interpreted in terms of human behavior.

**Q: What two key questions must a zooarchaeologist address before interpreting behavior from bones?**
A: First, were the bones deposited by humans (rather than carnivores, raptors, or natural death)? Second, were they deposited during the same occupation of the site? Evidence used to answer these questions includes stone tool cut marks (vs. carnivore tooth marks), burning, impact fractures, association with hearths, anatomical position, and differential weathering.

**Q: What are the two steps in identifying an animal bone, and what does each require?**
A: The first step is assigning each specimen to an **element** — the anatomical body part (femur, rib, skull fragment, etc.). The second step is identifying it to **taxon** — the kind of animal, ideally to species. Both steps require hands-on familiarity with a **comparative collection** of modern animal skeletons.

**Q: What are animal size classes, and when are they used?**
A: When fragments are too small or ambiguous to identify to species or family, analysts assign them to one of five size classes based on body mass. Size class 1 = rodents and rabbits; 2 = wolf/pronghorn; 3 = mule deer/bighorn sheep; 4 = bison/elk; 5 = giraffes, hippos, elephants. It is the option of last resort — analysts always prefer species-level identification.

**Q: What is a comparative collection, and how is one built?**
A: A reference library of modern animal skeletons, labeled by species, age, sex, and collection data. Built by hunting, trapping, collecting road kills (with appropriate licenses), or acquiring carcasses from wildlife agencies. Specimens must be defleshed — by burial, simmering in detergent, or using dermestid beetle colonies — before they can be curated.

---

## Part 2: Counting Bones — NISP and MNI

**Q: What is NISP, and what is its main limitation?**
A: NISP (Number of Identified Specimens) is the total count of bone specimens identified to a particular taxon. It is easy to compute and good for comparing many assemblages across sites. Its limitation: a single highly fragmented skeleton could produce hundreds of NISP, inflating the apparent importance of that species relative to others.

**Q: What is MNI, and how is it calculated?**
A: MNI (Minimum Number of Individuals) is the minimum number of individual animals needed to account for all skeletal elements of a species. It is calculated by tabulating bone frequency by element and side (left vs. right), then taking the count of the most abundant element. Analysts compare fragments to check whether any could belong to the same individual, reducing the MNI accordingly.

**Q: What are the limitations of MNI?**
A: MNI depends heavily on how the site is divided: a coarse-grained, repeatedly occupied site might yield an MNI of 1 or 2 despite hundreds of fragments. It is also sensitive to fragmentation — highly broken bones are harder to match. MNI is most reliable in fine-grained assemblages with minimal fragmentation and fine stratigraphic divisions.

**Q: What is a fine-grained versus a coarse-grained assemblage?**
A: Fine-grained: the assemblage results from one or a few short occupations (like Agate Basin's Folsom component). Coarse-grained: the assemblage accumulated over many overlapping occupations across decades or centuries. MNI is most meaningful in fine-grained contexts.

---

## Part 3: The Agate Basin Case Study (Faunal Analysis in Action)

**Q: What did Matt Hill conclude about the Agate Basin Folsom component, and when was the site occupied?**
A: About 10,780 radiocarbon years ago, a small group of Folsom hunters camped at Agate Basin in late March or early April. They killed at least 11 *Bison antiquus* and 5 pronghorn antelope. Bison were partially butchered at the kill site; antelope were field-dressed and carried back nearly whole. The hunters appear to have lacked stored food and were hunting on a weekly or daily basis.

**Q: How did Hill determine the season of occupation?**
A: By analyzing tooth eruption and bone development in juvenile bison, which follow predictable developmental schedules. Two young animals were about 11 and 23 months old, placing their deaths in late March–early April (given that modern bison calve in late April–early May). Fetal bison bones corroborated a late-winter/early-spring occupation. This inference rests on a uniformitarian assumption: ancient bison birthing seasons closely approximated modern ones.

**Q: What does the pattern of missing axial skeleton elements at Agate Basin tell us?**
A: The axial skeleton (skull, vertebrae, ribs, sacrum) is rare compared to appendicular elements (limb bones). Since limb bones carry more meat and marrow than axial elements, this pattern indicates **selective transport**: hunters brought high-utility portions to camp and left low-utility axial elements at the kill site.

**Q: Why did Hill interpret the cracked toe bones (metapodials and phalanges) as evidence of food stress?**
A: Foot bones contain little marrow and have no meat. Processing them makes sense only when people are short on better food. Hill also notes that Folsom hunters seem not to have stored food — unlike later Plains hunter-gatherers — making the lean late winter/early spring period especially difficult.

---

## Part 4: Chavín de Huántar — Faunal Analysis and the Ch'arki Effect

**Q: What is Chavín de Huántar, and why is it a useful faunal case study?**
A: A major Andean ceremonial center (~3150–2450 BP) at 3150 m elevation in Peru. George Miller and Richard Burger analyzed ~2000 identified specimens across three temporal phases to track how subsistence changed from mixed hunting/herding to near-exclusive llama pastoralism, using both taxonomic and element-level data.

**Q: What are the four Andean camelids, and which are domesticated?**
A: Llama (domesticated; mainly used as a pack animal), alpaca (domesticated; valued for fine fiber), guanaco (wild; hunted for meat), vicuña (wild; hunted for fine hair). At Chavín de Huántar, llama and vicuña bones were most common.

**Q: What is the ch'arki effect, and why is it archaeologically important?**
A: Ch'arki is llama/alpaca meat freeze-dried on the bone in the high-altitude puna (the native Quechua word entered English as "jerky"). Head and foot bones are cut off during production and discarded at the puna herding site. The ch'arki chunks — with leg bones and vertebrae still attached — travel to valley trade partners. Result: puna sites accumulate head/foot bones; receiving sites accumulate leg/vertebra bones. Knowing this allows archaeologists to reconstruct trade networks from bone distributions alone.

**Q: How did the faunal assemblage at Chavín de Huántar change across the three phases?**
A: Urabarriu phase (earliest, 2950–2750 BP): mixed hunting and herding; camelid remains mostly head and foot bones, suggesting ch'arki was being produced locally or received from nearby puna. Chakinani phase (2750–2650 BP): deer drops off sharply; camelid rises to 83%. Janabarriu phase (2650–2350 BP): camelid at 93% of assemblage; leg bones now dominant — a clear ch'arki-receiving signature. The site had shifted from local self-sufficiency to long-distance trade dependence.

## Part 5: Plant Remains — Methods Overview

**Q: What are macrobotanical remains, and how are they recovered?**
A: Readily recognizable plant parts: seeds, charcoal, corncobs, nut hulls, etc. Best preserved in arid climates or when carbonized by fire. Recovered primarily through **flotation** (water separation of light plant material from heavy sediment), but also found in waterlogged contexts, adobe, coprolites, and ceramic interiors.

**Q: What is palynology, and why is pollen well preserved?**
A: Palynology is the analysis of ancient plant pollen and spores. Pollen grains are microscopic male gametes that plants shed in enormous quantities into the atmosphere. They are morphologically distinctive (identifiable to genus or species under a microscope) and chemically resistant — surviving hydrofluoric acid baths in the lab — allowing preservation for tens of thousands of years under the right conditions.

**Q: What is the sampling protocol for pollen in a stratigraphic profile?**
A: The profile face is scraped clean to expose fresh sediment. Samples of ~0.2–0.3 L are collected from bottom to top (not top to bottom) at 5–10 cm intervals to prevent contamination from falling dirt. Samples are sealed in sterile containers. In the lab, pollen is isolated via repeated hydrofluoric acid baths and centrifuging, mounted on slides, and scanned at 400–1000×.

**Q: How is a pollen diagram read, and what does it tell us?**
A: The diagram plots plant taxon percentages against stratigraphic levels, read from bottom (oldest) to top (most recent). Changes in pollen frequencies reflect changes in plant density and, by extension, past environments. You must distinguish local from regional signals: a spring in a desert may produce forest pollen that doesn't reflect regional vegetation.

**Q: What are phytoliths, and how do they differ from pollen as evidence?**
A: Phytoliths are microscopic silica bodies ("plant stones") deposited inside plant cells. Nearly indestructible, they persist for millions of years. Unlike pollen — which travels on wind and represents both local and regional plants — phytoliths are deposited where the plant grew or decayed, making them a highly **local** signal. Most useful for identifying grass and sedge species.

## Part 6: Case Studies in Plant Analysis

**Q: What did the three-part analysis at Stillwater Marsh (26CH1062, Nevada) conclude about bulrush seeds?**
A: Flotation recovered carbonized bulrush seeds (local food remains). Phytolith analysis found *no* sedge phytoliths — so no bulrush plants decayed on the site. Pollen analysis showed bulrush was abundant in the surrounding marsh. Conclusion: people brought bulrush seeds *to* the site as food, rather than using bulrush plants as building or craft material on-site.

**Q: What is the significance of the *absent* seeds at Stillwater Marsh?**
A: No upland plant seeds — piñon pine hulls, ricegrass — were found. This suggests people living in the Carson Desert obtained all their plant food from the wetland and did not travel even short distances into the foothills to gather upland resources.

**Q: What are wood rat (pack rat) middens, and what makes them valuable to paleoecology?**
A: Ancient Neotoma nests built in rock crevices and sheltered overhangs. Rats collect materials within ~100 m of the nest, so the assemblage represents a highly local sample. Urine lacquer on the nest preserves organic material — twigs, seeds, cones — for thousands of years. Radiocarbon dating of successive levels produces a detailed record of local vegetational change.

**Q: How did wood rat midden data from Nevada change the interpretation of Stillwater Marsh sites?**
A: Peter Wigand and Cheryl Nowak found that piñon pine evidence doesn't appear in middens near the Stillwater Range until ~1200 BP — *after* the marsh sites were occupied. So people weren't bypassing piñon in favor of marsh resources; piñon simply wasn't present in sufficient abundance to make a trip worthwhile. Environmental context is essential for interpreting subsistence choices.

**Q: What is lipid analysis, and what can it reveal?**
A: Extraction of fats, oils, and waxes (lipids) from pottery walls or sediments using chloroform/methanol, followed by gas chromatography and mass spectrometry. Because lipids absorb into ceramic walls during cooking and resist water, they survive long after other food traces disappear. Richard Evershed (University of Bristol) has identified vegetable, milk, and meat residues in European pottery and demonstrated that fat distribution within vessel walls matches the physics of cooking (fats rise to the top).

## Part 7: Beyond Economics — The Symbolic Meaning of Plants

**Q: What did Hastorf and Johannessen find in the Upper Mantaro Valley (Peru), and why is it significant?**
A: Analyzing charcoal from ~900 flotation samples spanning 1000 years (~1500–500 BP), they found that mature wood use declined through the Pancán phases (population growth → fuel scarcity) but then *increased* during Wanka II and III, against ecological expectations. Fuel management/tree cultivation seems to have been adopted — but not simply for economic reasons.

**Q: What symbolic role did trees play in Andean cosmology, and how does this complicate the economic explanation?**
A: Certain trees were planted at administrative sites and linked to deities. *Quishuar* wood was burned in rituals and used to carve human figures sacrificed to Inca ancestors. Trees symbolized family continuity, ancestors (roots), and children (fruit). A ritual step in marriage ceremonies was called "bringing the branch." Hastorf and Johannessen argue that the choice to plant trees — one of several solutions to fuel scarcity — reflects these cultural values, not just economic logic. This is a postprocessual insight: material evidence can carry symbolic meaning that purely ecological analysis misses.

# Chapter 10 Study Guide
## Bioarchaeological Approaches to the Past
### ANTH 103 · Kelly & Thomas, *Archaeology* 7th ed.

---

## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Chapter 10 turns from artifacts and sites to the human body itself. **Bioarchaeology** — the study of the human biological component of the archaeological record — uses bone, bone chemistry, and DNA to reconstruct disease, diet, workload, and population history. The chapter builds around two case studies led by bioarchaeologist Clark Larsen: the hunter-gatherers of **Stillwater Marsh** (Nevada) and the early farmers of **Çatalhöyük** (Turkey). Together they let Larsen test a long-standing anthropological debate — were pre-agricultural lives "nasty, brutish, and short," or something closer to an "original affluent society"? The chapter closes by moving from bone to **molecular archaeology**: how ancient and modern DNA are used to trace human migrations, test the "African Eve" hypothesis, and investigate the relationship between Neanderthals and modern humans.

---

## Part 1: Skeletal Analysis Basics

**Q: What is osteology, and what is a burial population?**
A: Osteology is the study of bone. A burial population is a set of human burials from a limited region and time period — the more limited the region and time span, the more reliable the inferences drawn from it. The Stillwater burials, spread across a 16-square-kilometer marsh with few dated or artifact-associated burials, made this an imperfect burial population to work with.

**Q: How do bioarchaeologists determine sex from a skeleton?**
A: Primarily from the pelvis: the **sciatic notch** (a U-shaped indentation in the os coxa) is wider in women, and the **subpubic angle** is wider (obtuse) in women and more acute in men. Because the porous os coxae decay easily, bioarchaeologists often turn to the skull instead — male skulls tend to be more robust, with heavier brow ridges, larger mastoid processes, more rugged muscle attachments, and squarer chins/eye orbits. Sex is very difficult to determine in children, since these traits don't develop until young adulthood.

**Q: How do bioarchaeologists determine age at death?**
A: Multiple methods, layered by age range:
- **Teeth** (best for subadults): crown formation and eruption timing are consistent across populations.
- **Epiphyseal fusion**: the ends (epiphyses) of long bones fuse to the shaft at known ages (e.g., proximal radius by ~19, scapula portions by ~23); most bones are fused by 25.
- **Pubic symphysis degeneration**: the joint where the os coxae meet changes appearance in a regular way from age 20 (ridged) to 35 (rim forms) to 50 (looks like a "shriveled prune").
- **Tooth wear**: useful but complicated by diet — grinding-stone-processed food increases wear rate.

**Q: What cultural practices complicate skeletal analysis?**
A: Charnel houses, used by many eastern Native American groups, let bodies decompose in the open before bones were cleaned and placed in communal **bundle burials** — commingling remains from multiple individuals. At Stillwater, postdepositional scattering meant only 54 of 500 recovered individuals were intact primary burials.

---

## Part 2: How Well Did the Stillwater People Live?

**Q: What research question drove Larsen's Stillwater study?**
A: Whether hunter-gatherer life was Thomas Hobbes's "nasty, brutish, and short," or Marshall Sahlins's "original affluent society" — a debate rooted in conflicting ethnographic accounts (including 1960s research on the Ju/'hoansi of the Kalahari). Larsen used **paleopathology** (the study of ancient disease, disorders, and trauma) to bring skeletal evidence to bear on the question.

**Q: What did Larsen find regarding disease and trauma at Stillwater?**
A: No evidence of syphilis, tuberculosis, or leprosy. Some evidence of iron deficiency anemia, visible as **porotic hyperostosis** (spongy, porous cranial bone) or **cribra orbitalia** (the same effect in the eye sockets) — but only in four burials. Physical trauma was also rare: just 18 individuals had healed bone breaks, 6 of them broken noses.

**Q: What are growth arrest features, and what do they reveal about childhood?**
A: **Harris lines** are thin lines of bone visible on X-ray, formed when long-bone growth is interrupted by disease, trauma, or malnutrition. **Enamel hypoplasias** are grooves in tooth enamel formed the same way, but — unlike Harris lines — they remain visible for life once formed, since teeth aren't remodeled the way bone is.

**Q: How did Larsen measure workload at Stillwater?**
A: Through **osteoarthritis** — a joint disorder from cartilage loss caused by mechanical stress, visible as bony lipping (**osteophytes**) or polished, worn joint surfaces (**eburnation**). Every single adult skeleton at Stillwater showed osteoarthritis in at least one joint — the most severe Larsen had ever observed. He also used **long bone cross sections** (with Christopher Ruff) from CAT scans: bones under heavy mechanical stress develop more oval cross sections. Both lines of evidence showed men did more, and more strenuous, walking than women, while women showed more lower-back osteoarthritis — likely from carrying children and grinding seeds on metates.

**Q: What is paleodemography, and what did the Stillwater mortality profile show?**
A: Paleodemography reconstructs life expectancy, age structure, and age-at-death patterns using **mortality profiles** built from burial age/sex data. The Stillwater profile showed very high child mortality (consistent with the 50–60% child mortality typical of foraging populations), a rise in female deaths starting at the early childbearing years, and very few people surviving past 50 — a 45-year-old at Stillwater was effectively an elder.

**Q: How do isotopes reveal ancient diet?**
A: Carbon and nitrogen stable isotopes are preserved in **bone collagen** (the organic component of bone). Carbon isotope ratios distinguish maize-heavy diets (like Pecos Pueblo) from meat-heavy diets (like Ontario foragers); nitrogen isotope ratios rise with more carnivorous diets, and further rise with marine food consumption. Margaret Schoeninger's analysis placed Stillwater between the two extremes — a mixed marsh-based diet of plants and animals, with more year-to-year dietary variability than either comparison population.

**Q: What was Larsen's final answer to the "affluence vs. nasty/brutish" question?**
A: "Both and neither." Stillwater people were relatively healthy, well-fed, and largely disease-free — supporting the affluent-society model. But they also worked hard, suffered joint pain, endured periods of malnutrition, and rarely lived past 50 — supporting the Hobbesian model. The takeaway is that the past resists simple, one-sided characterizations.

## Part 3: How Well Did the Çatalhöyük People Live?

**Q: What is Çatalhöyük, and why is it useful for studying the forager-to-farmer transition?**
A: A major Neolithic site in Turkey (occupied roughly 7100–6000 BC), first excavated by James Mellaart in the 1960s, then by Ian Hodder starting in the 1990s (Larsen joined in 2003). Its long occupation and dense, well-preserved mudbrick architecture make it ideal for tracking how the shift to farming affected health and lifestyle over time.

**Q: What did paleodemography reveal about Çatalhöyük's growth?**
A: By computing the age structure of the juvenile population (ages 3–19), Larsen found a dramatic birthrate increase in the early occupation that continued through the middle period, then dropped in the late period — confirmed independently by house-count data. This indicates population growth came from increased fertility, not just in-migration.

**Q: How did diet and dental health compare to Stillwater?**
A: Everyone at Çatalhöyük ate cereal grains (wheat, barley) with sheep/cattle protein, with no significant male/female dietary difference. Because starchy diets promote **dental caries** (cavities caused by bacteria metabolizing carbohydrates into enamel-dissolving acid), Çatalhöyük had more cavities than Stillwater's low-carbohydrate foragers (Stillwater: only ~3% of skeletons had caries) — though not extreme numbers.

**Q: How did workload and stature compare to Stillwater?**
A: Çatalhöyük showed less severe and more evenly distributed degenerative joint disease than Stillwater, with work conditions apparently improving toward the late period, though some individuals suffered more than others — suggesting emerging inequality. Stature comparisons with Denver and Native American farming populations showed similar early growth trajectories, indicating Çatalhöyük children had access to adequate nutrition despite crowded living conditions.

**Q: What is biodistance analysis, and what did it reveal about Çatalhöyük households?**
A: A statistical method using standardized dental traits (which correlate with genetic variability) to estimate biological relatedness among burials. Larsen expected people buried beneath a house floor to be biological relatives, but found biological affinity played only a limited role — suggesting Çatalhöyük households were often organized around **fictive ("practical") kin** rather than biological kin, likely for shared economic tasks like herding and farming.

**Q: What are "history houses"?**
A: More elaborate Çatalhöyük dwellings, rebuilt across multiple generations and decorated with relief sculpture and wall paintings of hunters and wild animals (leopards, boars, bulls, deer) — despite wild game contributing almost nothing to the actual diet, showing a split between economic practice and symbolic/ritual life.

## Part 4: Archaeology and DNA

**Q: What is molecular archaeology?**
A: The use of genetic material — from ancient skeletal remains and living populations — to reconstruct the past, joining geneticists and archaeologists in a relatively new interdisciplinary approach.

**Q: What's the difference between nuclear DNA and mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) for archaeological purposes?**
A: **Nuclear DNA** carries the bulk of genetic information (~3 billion base pairs, ~50,000 genes) but degrades quickly after death, limiting its archaeological usefulness. **mtDNA**, found outside the nucleus in mitochondria, is far less abundant (about 0.0006% of nuclear DNA) but survives and is recoverable more easily because each cell holds thousands of copies (recovered via **PCR**, which amplifies tiny DNA samples). mtDNA is inherited only from the mother, doesn't recombine, and mutates 6–16 times faster than nuclear DNA — making it useful as a molecular "clock" for dating population splits.

**Q: What is aDNA, and what challenges come with recovering it?**
A: Ancient DNA recovered from archaeological organic remains. Pioneering work includes Allan Wilson and Vince Sarich's 1984 cloning of DNA from 140-year-old quagga skin, and Svante Pääbo's cloning of DNA from a 4,400-year-old Egyptian mummy — the first application of PCR to ancient humans. The central challenge is contamination by modern human DNA, requiring strict lab protocols and cross-lab replication to confirm results.

**Q: What is the "African Eve" hypothesis?**
A: Wilson, Rebecca Cann, and Mark Stoneking compared mtDNA from 147 people worldwide and found all living humans share all but about 0.6% of their mtDNA — with African populations showing the greatest internal diversity and the "longest branch" on the resulting family tree. Because mtDNA passes only through the maternal line, they inferred a single common female ancestor (nicknamed "Eve") dating to roughly 200,000 years ago, supporting the "out of Africa" model of modern human origins. This is disputed by researchers (e.g., Milford Wolpoff) who see skeletal continuity across regions arguing against a total replacement model.

**Q: What has DNA evidence revealed about Neanderthals?**
A: Neanderthals lived in Europe by at least 400,000 years ago and coexisted with modern humans in the Near East for millennia before disappearing in Europe by about 40,000 years ago. Early mtDNA studies found no overlap with modern humans, but Svante Pääbo's 2010 team sequenced roughly two-thirds of the nuclear Neanderthal genome and found no Neanderthal DNA in sub-Saharan Africans, but 1–4% in other populations — evidence of limited interbreeding roughly 40,000–50,000 years ago, likely in the Near East. Pääbo's team also found Neanderthals carried the gene linked to human speech capability, though whether they had full symbolic/metaphorical language remains unknown.

# Chapter 11 Study Guide
## Reconstructing Social and Political Systems of the Past
### ANTH 103 · Kelly & Thomas, *Archaeology* 7th ed.

---

## Big Picture: What This Chapter Is About

Chapter 11 moves from diet and skeletons into harder territory: reconstructing **gender, kinship, social status, and trade** — the invisible scaffolding of past societies. This is difficult because artifacts aren't just functional tools; they're also **symbols**, and archaeologists usually can't tell whether a pattern reflects behavior, meaning, or both. Because middle-level theory is thin here, archaeologists lean heavily on **ethnographic analogy**, cross-cultural comparison (the Embers' worldwide correlations), and increasingly on hard science — stable isotopes, biodistance studies, aDNA, and geochemical "fingerprinting" (XRF, INAA, petrography) — to test hypotheses about social and political organization that once relied on guesswork alone.

---

## Part 1: Social Vocabulary & Artifacts as Symbols

**Q: What is social organization, and how does it differ from political organization?**
A: **Social organization** is the rules and structures governing relationships within a group of interacting people — divided into social units (groups) with recognized positions (statuses) and prescribed behaviors (roles). **Political organization** is the formal and informal institutions that regulate a society's *collective* acts. A group can have rich social organization (kinship, gender roles) with very little formal political organization, as among the Great Basin Shoshone.

**Q: What is the difference between residential and nonresidential groups?**
A: **Residential groups** (households, bands, villages) are physical, face-to-face associations that regulate discrete spatial matters. **Nonresidential groups** are abstract — they may never physically convene — and are manifested archaeologically through symbols, ceremonies, mythologies, or stylistic insignia (e.g., ceramic decoration, architecture, rock art). Nonresidential groups bind residential units together across a landscape.

**Q: Contrast the 19th-century Great Basin Shoshone with 18th-century Tahitian society.**
A: The Shoshone lived in ephemeral nuclear-family clusters with authority limited to temporary, task-specific leadership (e.g., a skilled hunter directing a communal antelope drive) — no formal political integration above the family. Tahiti, by contrast, had ~20 competing **chiefdoms**, each ruled by a hereditary "sacred chief" claiming descent from the gods, with small chiefs, sub-chiefs, and commoners below — a rigid, ascribed hierarchy that controlled land, canoes, craft specialists, and tribute redistribution.

**Q: Why do archaeologists say artifacts are "not just things"?**
A: Because material culture carries **symbolic** as well as functional meaning, and it's often impossible to tell which dimension is operating. Bringing wine to dinner as a thank-you gesture is meaningless to the Mikea, who expect tobacco instead — the same object category (a gift) carries entirely different cultural meanings.

## Part 2: Archaeology and Gender

**Q: Distinguish sex, gender, gender role, and gender ideology.**
A:
- **Sex**: inherited, biological differences between males and females (only two).
- **Gender**: culturally constructed ideas about sex differences (can exceed two — e.g., Plains Indian **berdaches**/"two-spirits," men who lived as women and could marry men).
- **Gender role**: differential participation of men and women in social, economic, political, and religious institutions — culturally appropriate behavior.
- **Gender ideology**: the culturally specific value assigned to "male," "female," "sex," and "reproduction" — how equally (or unequally) genders are regarded.

**Q: What is androcentric bias, and how did Conkey and Spector identify it in archaeology?**
A: **Androcentric bias** is viewing the world through men's activities and perceptions — historically, through white, middle-class, European male assumptions. Margaret Conkey and Janet Spector argued that mostly-male archaeologists rarely examined or validated these assumptions, so the field's "middle-level theory" was often just unexamined cultural bias.

**Q: What happened at Indian Knoll (Kentucky), and what did it reveal about gendered assumptions?**
A: A 2.5-meter-deep shell midden and burial ground (~6100–4500 bp) excavated by C. B. Moore and William Webb, who recovered ~1200 burials. Men were typically buried with axes and fishhooks; women with beads, mortars, and pestles. Webb identified enigmatic polished stones and antler hooks as **atlatl** (spear-thrower) parts — but was puzzled that 13 of 76 atlatl-bearing burials were adult women and 18 were children. Because Webb could not conceive of women as hunters, he explained this away as symbolic mourning ritual rather than considering that women may have hunted. This illustrates how unexamined cultural bias can shape (and limit) archaeological interpretation.

**Q: How did Bailey and Aunger resolve the puzzle of BaMbuti net-hunting vs. bow-hunting?**
A: Rather than cultural preference, environment, or trade distance (all of which they ruled out), the real driver was **women's labor decisions**: where Bantu horticulturalists had small gardens (and didn't need BaMbuti labor), BaMbuti women helped hunt with nets; where Bantu gardens were large, BaMbuti women worked those fields for produce instead, leaving men to hunt individually with bows and arrows.

**Q: How did Prudence Rice link pottery-making technique to gender?**
A: Cross-cultural survey found hand-built pottery is usually made by women; wheel-thrown pottery is usually made by men. The wheel is associated with craft specialization and marketing — so when pottery production shifts from the residential group to the nonresidential (market) group, the task tends to shift from women to men. This is a strong empirical analogy but a weak explanatory one (no inherent reason women can't use a wheel).

**Q: How did Rosemary Joyce use Maya iconography to study gender?**
A: Maya art rarely shows overt sexual characteristics (elaborate costuming obscures them), so Joyce identified women by dress style (wrapped garment, huipil, or jade-bead skirt) in carvings, stelae, and painted pottery. Women are shown holding ceramic vessels, cloth bundles, or bloodletting instruments; men hold weapons and axe-scepters. Drawing on the **cargo system** (in which a man's wife serves as ceremonial "Mother of" his title), Joyce argued that paired male/female figures on stelae may represent complementary — not subservient — roles, and that rulers needed to symbolically combine male and female elements to claim full political power.

## Part 3: Archaeology and Kinship

**Q: What is kinship, and why does it matter more in nonindustrial societies?**
A: **Kinship** is the socially recognized network of relationships linking individuals through descent (real or imagined) and marriage. In nonindustrial societies, most daily interaction is with kin, so misunderstanding a kinship system means misreading behavior — as with the Pingelap woman whose "father" was actually her mother's brother (a matrilineal "fictive" father).

**Q: Compare bilateral, patrilineal, and matrilineal descent.**
A:
- **Bilateral descent**: relatives traced equally through both parents (e.g., most industrialized nations); the nuclear family is the key economic unit; kinship tends to lack "depth."
- **Patrilineal descent**: ancestry/lineage traced through the male line (**patrilineage**); ~60% of world societies; associated with hunting-gathering, agricultural, and pastoral economies and internal warfare (with close neighbors); the lineage, not the nuclear family, controls land and resources.
- **Matrilineal descent**: ancestry/lineage traced through the female line (**matrilineage**); only ~10% of world societies; associated with horticulture, long-distance hunting, and warfare with distant enemies. The mother's brother functions as a social "father," since children belong to their mother's lineage.

**Q: What are clans and moieties?**
A: A **clan** is a set of lineages claiming descent from a shared (often mythical) ancestor. **Moieties** are the two halves a society is divided into when clans are grouped in pairs; moieties often perform reciprocal ceremonial obligations for each other (e.g., burying each other's dead, hosting feasts).

**Q: How do patrilocal and matrilocal residence appear archaeologically? (the Embers' cross-cultural data)**
A: **Patrilocal residence** (bride moves to groom's village) correlates with smaller houses (<60 sq. m), since the nuclear family is the residential unit — as at Mikea forest hamlets. **Matrilocal residence** (groom moves to bride's village) correlates with larger houses (>100 sq. m), since sisters and their families often share one large subdivided house (e.g., Iroquois longhouses) — likely reflecting cooperative bonds among sisters while husbands are away at long-distance hunting or warfare.

**Q: How did Peregrine and later researchers evaluate matrilocal residence at Chaco Canyon?**
A: Peter Peregrine argued that Chaco's shift from small pithouses (~15 sq. m) to clustered small-pueblo rooms (70–300+ sq. m combined) paralleled the Embers' matrilocal pattern. But Schillaci and Stojanowski's biodistance analysis of Pueblo Bonito burials found *greater* genetic variation among females than males — the opposite of what matrilocal residence predicts — suggesting patrilocal or bilocal residence instead. Gina Jorgenson's strontium isotope study found that before ~3500 years ago, men's tooth enamel (childhood signature) differed from their bone (adult) signature while women's matched — consistent with matrilocal postmarital residence — a pattern that broke down as the population became more sedentary.

**Q: How did James Knight use the Chickasaw to interpret kinship at Moundville?**
A: Knight used 19th-century Chickasaw village layout (houses arranged around a square plaza, organized into matrilocal clans grouped into two moieties on opposite sides) as an ethnographic analogy for Moundville's eight paired residential/burial mound groups arranged around its plaza. Key difference: Chickasaw villages had no central mound or paramount chief, while Moundville's large Mound B likely represents a chiefly residence — a political tier absent from the ethnographic analogy.

## Part 4: Archaeology and Social Status

**Q: Define status, ascribed status, and achieved status.**
A: **Status** is the rights, duties, privileges, and powers attached to a recognized, named social position. **Ascribed status** is assigned at birth regardless of ability (e.g., a hereditary chief or prince). **Achieved status** is earned through individual accomplishment (e.g., a Shoshone man's temporary status as a skilled hunting leader) and is not automatically inherited.

**Q: Distinguish egalitarian and ranked societies.**
A: **Egalitarian societies** (small-scale versions called bands) have roughly as many valued statuses as people capable of filling them; access to critical resources is roughly equal; leadership is situational, based on experience, not inherited. **Ranked societies** restrict valued positions so not everyone can achieve them; they feature hierarchy, unequal access to resources, and economies that redistribute goods through a ranked elite who keep a portion for themselves (e.g., Tahiti, Northwest Coast tribes, Moundville).

**Q: What is the Southeastern Ceremonial Complex, and what does it signal?**
A: A set of stylistically similar high-status Mississippian-period artifacts (conch shell gorgets/cups, copper plates, ceremonial axes, effigy pipes) bearing shared motifs (forked eye, sun circle, hand-and-eye, bilobed arrow) found across sites from Oklahoma to Florida. Local symbols at a site like Moundville marked status *within* that community, while Ceremonial Complex symbols marked rank across the wider region — evidence of social interaction beyond simple trade.

**Q: How did Peebles and Kus reconstruct ranking at Moundville?**
A: Statistical analysis of grave goods from 2053 burials revealed a pyramid-shaped status structure with a **superordinate division** (largely inherited/ascribed rank — including richly furnished infant and child burials, proving status could be ascribed at birth) and a **subordinate division** (status tied to age and sex, achieved over a lifetime). The supreme cluster (Cluster IA) — seven elite adult males buried in Mounds C and D with copper axes and sacrificial retainers — likely represents paramount chiefs.

**Q: Did Moundville's elite actually live better than commoners?**
A: Partially. Faunal analysis (Jackson & Scott) showed the elite ate choicer cuts and imported game (bison, shark). Bone chemistry (Schoeninger) showed high-status men ate more meat than low-status men or women, though differences were modest. But Mary Lucas Powell found **no significant health differences** (height, disease, trauma) between elite and commoner skeletons — status brought better food access, but commoners were not living in want.

## Part 5: Trade and Political Organization

**Q: What are exotics, and why do elites trade or give them away?**
A: **Exotics** are artifacts made of raw material or in a style signaling contact with a distant region. As societies shift from egalitarian bands to ranked societies, exotics become central to competitive feasting (e.g., the potlatch) — visual symbols of an elite person's far-reaching social, political, or religious connections and power.

**Q: Contrast direct acquisition and down-the-line trade.**
A: **Direct acquisition**: a person/group travels to the source itself to extract, trade for, or receive a raw material or artifact as a gift. **Down-the-line trade**: material passes from neighbor to neighbor, progressively farther from the source, usually declining in frequency with distance — though an unexpected spike far from the source can signal a secondary trading center.

**Q: How does XRF trace obsidian to its geologic source?**
A: **Energy dispersive X-ray fluorescence (XRF)** measures the trace-element "fingerprint" (zinc, rubidium, strontium, barium, etc.) of an obsidian sample — unique to each lava flow because each flow forms in a single geologic instant. It's nondestructive, works on samples as small as 1mm, and takes minutes. Richard Hughes used XRF to trace Hopewell mound obsidian (Ohio/Illinois/Indiana, 2200–1600 bp) to Obsidian Cliff (Yellowstone) and Bear Gulch (Idaho) — evidence of down-the-line trade linking distant Hopewell communities.

**Q: How do INAA and petrographic analysis trace ceramics to their source?**
A: **Petrographic analysis** cuts a thin (~30-micron) pottery section and identifies mineral composition (temper and clay) under a polarizing microscope. **Instrumental neutron activation analysis (INAA)** bombards a sherd with neutrons, then measures the gamma radiation emitted as induced isotopes decay, identifying 30+ trace elements (destructive, but sherds are usually abundant). William Dickinson's petrographic work and Descantes, Neff, and Glascock's INAA study both traced Micronesian atoll pottery (temper and clay) back to the high island of **Yap**, confirming a "down-the-line"–style exchange network (**sawei**) linking Yap, Palau, and outlying atolls.

**Q: What was the Yap sawei trade system, and what did it exchange?**
A: A political-economic network in which outlying atolls sent woven mats, sennit rope, and shell valuables to Ulithi, which forwarded them to Yap; Yap sent back taro, yams, sweet potatoes, bananas, and canoe timber. Yap also imported large stone disks (**rai**, "stone money") from Palau for competitive feasting. Scholars debate whether sawei was an equal exchange or a tribute system, but atolls appear to have received more material support than they gave — and could take refuge on Yap during storms or drought.
