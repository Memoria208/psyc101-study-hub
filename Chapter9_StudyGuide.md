## Part 1: Motivational Concepts

**Q: What is motivation, and what three perspectives help explain motivated behavior?**
A: Motivation is a need or desire that energizes and directs behavior, arising from the interplay of nature (the bodily "push") and nurture (the "pulls" of experience and culture). Three key perspectives are drive-reduction theory, arousal theory, and Maslow's hierarchy of needs.

**Q: What are the three assumptions of drive-reduction theory, and what is homeostasis?**
A: (1) We have physiological needs (e.g., food, water); (2) an unmet need creates a drive, an aroused motivated state; (3) that drive pushes us to reduce the need. The goal is homeostasis — the body's tendency to maintain a steady internal state, much like a thermostat.

**Q: How do incentives interact with drives?**
A: Incentives are environmental stimuli that pull us toward or away from a behavior, based on learning. When both a need and an incentive are present (e.g., hunger plus the smell of baking pizza), motivation is especially strong.

**Q: What does arousal theory explain that drive-reduction theory cannot?**
A: Arousal theory explains motivated behaviors that increase rather than decrease arousal — such as curiosity-driven exploration — even when no physiological need exists. The Yerkes-Dodson law states that moderate arousal produces optimal performance; too little leads to boredom, too much leads to impaired performance.

**Q: What is Maslow's hierarchy of needs, and what are its limits?**
A: A pyramid ranking human needs from physiological needs (base) through safety, love/belonging, esteem, self-actualization, and — added later — self-transcendence (top). Critics note exceptions (e.g., activists who sacrifice basic needs for a cause) and cultural differences in what predicts well-being (money matters more in poorer nations; social connection matters more in wealthier ones).

## Part 2: Hunger

**Q: What did Cannon and Washburn's balloon study reveal about hunger, and what showed stomach contractions aren't the whole story?**
A: Washburn's stomach contractions correlated with his self-reported hunger pangs, suggesting stomach activity is one hunger cue. But rats (and humans) with their stomachs removed still feel hunger, showing stomach pangs are not the only source.

**Q: How does the brain and body chemistry regulate hunger?**
A: The hypothalamus monitors blood glucose and other signals. Appetite-stimulating and appetite-suppressing centers in the hypothalamus (including the arcuate nucleus) raise or lower hunger. Key hormones: ghrelin (stomach; increases appetite), orexin (hypothalamus; increases appetite), leptin (fat cells; decreases appetite), and PYY (digestive tract; decreases appetite).

**Q: What is the difference between "set point" and "settling point"?**
A: Set point is the theorized stable weight the body is regulated to defend, partly via basal metabolic rate adjustments. Settling point is an alternative view: weight settles wherever caloric intake and energy expenditure — shaped by environment as well as biology — land it, allowing for more gradual, sustained change.

**Q: What psychological and cultural factors influence hunger and taste preference?**
A: Memory of a recent meal can trigger hunger even without a bodily need (shown in amnesic patients who repeatedly agreed to eat). Sweet and salty preferences are genetic and universal; other preferences (spice tolerance, aversions) are learned through culture, climate (hot climates favor bacteria-slowing spices), and illness associations.

**Q: What situational factors increase how much we eat?**
A: Eating with friends, larger portion sizes, greater food variety, and easy accessibility of food (e.g., items placed first in a lunch line) all increase intake.

## Part 3: The Need to Belong

**Q: What evidence supports a fundamental human need to belong?**
A: Social bonds boosted ancestral survival through protection and cooperation. Having a social identity boosts health and well-being. Self-determination theory holds that we strive to satisfy competence, autonomy, and relatedness. Divorce and separation predict lower happiness and higher mortality risk across large international studies.

**Q: What is ostracism, and how does it affect people?**
A: Ostracism is social exclusion — shunning, ghosting, the silent treatment, solitary confinement. It threatens the need to belong, activates brain regions associated with physical pain, and can increase aggression, self-defeating behavior, and suicidal thinking. Feeling loved does the opposite — it activates reward-related brain areas and dampens physical pain.

**Q: How does social networking affect well-being, and is the evidence about smartphones and mental health conclusive?**
A: Correlational and longitudinal studies show a small association between heavy (3+ hours/day) social media use and depression, anxiety, and self-harm risk, especially in teen girls. Experimental evidence (e.g., a Facebook deactivation study) is more mixed but leans toward benefits from reduced use. Researchers are still debating whether screen time itself, or the social-comparison and envy effects of social media specifically, is the bigger driver.

**Q: How can social networking promote narcissism?**
A: Narcissistic (self-important, self-promoting) people are especially active on social media, post more staged selfies, and retaliate more against criticism. Experimentally, spending time editing and explaining one's own online profile raised narcissism scores compared with a neutral task.

## Part 4: Achievement Motivation

**Q: What is achievement motivation, and what personal qualities predict achievement?**
A: Henry Murray defined achievement motivation as a desire for significant accomplishment, mastery, control, and high standards. Longitudinal research on gifted children found the most professionally successful adults were more ambitious, energetic, and persistent — not simply more intelligent. Self-discipline has outpredicted intelligence test scores for school performance in some studies.

**Q: What is grit, and how does it relate to achievement?**
A: Grit (Angela Duckworth) is passionate dedication and perseverance toward a long-term goal. Gritty individuals are more likely to avoid burnout, persist through setbacks, and log the extensive deliberate practice associated with expert performance (e.g., top violinists' practice hours).

**Q: What is the difference between intrinsic and extrinsic motivation, and when do extrinsic rewards backfire?**
A: Intrinsic motivation is doing something for its own inherent satisfaction; extrinsic motivation is doing something for an external reward or to avoid punishment. Excessive extrinsic rewards for an already-enjoyable task can undermine intrinsic motivation (e.g., paying children to read reduces their voluntary reading time). Rewards work best when they signal competence rather than function as a bribe.

**Q: What are effective, research-based strategies for achieving personal goals?**
A: Set SMART (specific, measurable, achievable, realistic, timely) goals; announce goals publicly; build a detailed action plan; use short-term rewards to support long-term goals; monitor and share progress; create a supportive environment that limits temptation; and repeat the behavior consistently until it becomes habit.

## Part 5: Theories of Emotion

**Q: What are the three components of an emotion?**
A: Bodily arousal, expressive behavior, and conscious experience (including cognitive interpretation) — all triggered by an appraisal of a situation.

**Q: How does the James-Lange theory explain the order of arousal and feeling?**
A: James-Lange proposes that our awareness of our body's physiological response comes first, and the emotional feeling follows — we feel afraid because we notice we are trembling, not the reverse.

**Q: How does the Cannon-Bard theory differ from James-Lange?**
A: Cannon-Bard proposes that the emotion-triggering stimulus simultaneously causes both physiological arousal and the conscious experience of emotion — they occur independently and at the same time, not one causing the other. Evidence from soldiers with spinal cord injuries (weaker emotion with less bodily feedback) complicates this, suggesting the body does still contribute to felt emotion.

**Q: What did Schachter and Singer's epinephrine experiment demonstrate about the two-factor theory?**
A: Emotion requires both physical arousal and a cognitive label. Participants who were told to expect arousal from an injection felt little emotion because they attributed the arousal to the drug. Those not told the true effect "caught" the emotion (joy or irritation) displayed by a confederate in the room, showing that ambiguous arousal gets labeled based on context.

**Q: How do Zajonc, LeDoux, and Lazarus describe the "two-track brain" for emotion?**
A: Zajonc and LeDoux argue that simple emotional reactions (likes, dislikes, fears) can travel a fast "low road" — from the thalamus directly to the amygdala — producing a reaction before conscious appraisal. More complex emotions travel a slower "high road" through the cortex for analysis. Lazarus countered that even fast reactions involve some (often unconscious) cognitive appraisal of whether a stimulus is safe or dangerous.

## Part 6: Embodied and Expressed Emotion

**Q: What are the basic emotions, and how are emotions categorized dimensionally?**
A: Most researchers agree on a core set including anger, fear, disgust, sadness, and happiness, with some proposing many more (up to 28). Emotions are also mapped along two dimensions: valence (positive to negative) and arousal (low to high).

**Q: What roles do the sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous systems play in emotion?**
A: In a crisis, the sympathetic division of the ANS mobilizes the body — raising heart rate, breathing, and blood sugar while slowing digestion — preparing for fight or flight. The parasympathetic division calms the body once the danger passes.

**Q: Can we tell emotions apart by body responses alone? What does distinguish them?**
A: Bodily responses (heart rate, perspiration) are similar across fear, anger, and sexual arousal, making them hard to distinguish physiologically. Facial expressions and brain activity patterns (e.g., more right frontal lobe activity with negative emotion, more left frontal activity with positive emotion) differ more clearly between emotions.

**Q: How well do people detect emotion and deception in others, and are there gender differences?**
A: People are generally skilled at reading nonverbal cues (a "thin slice" of behavior can reveal a lot) and are especially quick to detect threat-related expressions like anger. However, people are poor at detecting lies (only slightly better than chance). Women tend to outperform men at decoding emotion and report greater empathy, a pattern with both biological and cultural (power-related) explanations.

**Q: How does culture shape emotional expression?**
A: Culture establishes display rules for how much and which emotions are appropriate to show — for example, more expressive positivity is typical in individualist Western cultures than in some collectivist cultures. Some expressions (like a smile signaling happiness) show more cross-cultural agreement than others (like fear or anger), and people read expressions from their own culture most accurately.

**Q: What is the facial feedback effect, and what is the related behavior feedback effect?**
A: The facial feedback effect is the tendency for a facial expression to trigger or amplify the corresponding emotion (e.g., forcing a smile can create mild happiness; a forced frown can create mild anger). The behavior feedback effect extends this to posture and movement — walking with a bouncy, upright gait can lift mood, while slumped, shuffling steps can lower it.
