# Chapter 3: Developing Through the Life Span — Study Guide

---

## Major Developmental Issues

**Q: What are the three major issues developmental psychologists debate?**
A: (1) Nature and nurture — how genetic inheritance interacts with experience to shape development. (2) Continuity and stages — whether development is gradual and continuous or happens in distinct stages. (3) Stability and change — which traits persist through life and which change as we age.

---

## Genetics & Prenatal Development

**Q: What's the relationship between chromosomes, DNA, and genes?**
A: Chromosomes are threadlike structures made of DNA. DNA is the molecule containing genetic information. Genes are the biochemical units of heredity — segments of DNA that make up the chromosomes. The genome is the complete set of genetic instructions for an organism.

**Q: What is epigenetics?**
A: The study of how environments can influence gene expression without changing the underlying DNA sequence. Epigenetic marks are molecules that trigger or block that expression.

**Q: What are the three prenatal stages?**
A: Zygote (fertilized egg, first 2 weeks of rapid cell division) → embryo (2 to 8 weeks after fertilization) → fetus (9 weeks after conception until birth).

**Q: What is a teratogen, and what's an example?**
A: An agent — such as a chemical or virus — that can reach the embryo or fetus and cause harm. Fetal alcohol syndrome (FAS), caused by heavy maternal drinking during pregnancy, is a key example, producing physical and cognitive deficits.

**Q: What can newborns already do?**
A: They arrive with automatic reflexes that support survival — rooting, sucking, startle, and grasping — and can smell and hear well enough to begin learning immediately. They also show a preference for face-like images over similarly complex non-face images.

**Q: What's the difference between identical and fraternal twins?**
A: Identical (monozygotic) twins develop from a single fertilized egg that splits in two, making them genetically identical. Fraternal (dizygotic) twins develop from two separate fertilized eggs and are no more genetically similar than typical siblings.

## Infancy & Childhood: Physical and Cognitive Development

**Q: What is maturation?**
A: Biological growth processes that lead to orderly changes in behavior, largely independent of experience — though experience adjusts the course maturation sets.

**Q: How does the brain develop after birth?**
A: Neural networks grow rapidly, especially in the frontal lobes between ages 3–6. An enriched environment increases neural connections, while disuse weakens pathways. There are critical periods for some skills, but brain development doesn't end in childhood.

**Q: What is infantile amnesia?**
A: The inability to consciously remember events from before about age 3–4, even though infants are capable of learning and forming some memories during this period.

**Q: What are Piaget's core concepts of cognitive development?**
A: A schema is a concept or framework that organizes and interprets information. Assimilation is interpreting new experiences using an existing schema; accommodation is adjusting or creating a schema to fit new information that doesn't match.

**Q: What are Piaget's four stages of cognitive development?**
A: Sensorimotor (birth–2 yrs): experiencing the world through senses/actions; object permanence develops. Preoperational (2–6/7 yrs): using language and symbols, but egocentric and not yet capable of logical operations. Concrete operational (7–11 yrs): logical thinking about concrete events; grasps conservation. Formal operational (12+ yrs): abstract, hypothetical, "if...then" reasoning.

**Q: How has later research modified Piaget's theory?**
A: Today's researchers see cognitive development as more continuous than Piaget's distinct stages suggested, and view even young infants as more cognitively competent ("little scientists") than Piaget believed — object permanence and other milestones appear to unfold more gradually.

**Q: What did Vygotsky emphasize that Piaget didn't?**
A: The social and cultural context of cognitive development. Scaffolding — temporary support from a more knowledgeable person — helps a child reach a higher level of thinking than they could reach alone; language plays a key role as a building block for thought.

**Q: What is theory of mind?**
A: The ability to understand that other people have their own mental states — beliefs, intentions — that may differ from your own. Most children develop this between ages 3 and 4½.

## Social Development & Attachment

**Q: What is stranger anxiety?**
A: Fear of unfamiliar people, commonly displayed by infants starting around 8 months, once they've developed schemas for familiar faces.

**Q: What did Ainsworth's "strange situation" experiment reveal?**
A: By observing how infants responded to a caregiver's departures and returns, Ainsworth identified attachment styles: securely attached infants (~60%) explore comfortably with the caregiver present, show distress at separation, and seek contact on reunion. Insecurely attached infants are either anxious (clingy, upset) or avoidant (seem not to notice the caregiver's absence or return).

**Q: What did Harlow's monkey experiments show about attachment?**
A: Infant monkeys raised with artificial "mothers" preferred a soft cloth mother over a wire mother that provided food, and became terror-stricken in unfamiliar situations without a mother figure present — showing attachment is about comfort and contact, not just feeding.

**Q: What are the four parenting styles, and their typical outcomes?**
A: Authoritarian (coercive, demanding) — children tend to have lower self-esteem and social skill. Permissive (unrestraining) — children tend to be more aggressive and immature. Neglectful (uninvolved) — children tend to have poor academic and social outcomes. Authoritative (responsive and demanding, "confrontive") — children tend to show the highest self-esteem, self-reliance, and social competence.

## Adolescence

**Q: What physical brain changes mark adolescence?**
A: The frontal lobes (judgment, impulse control) mature more slowly than the emotional limbic system, which can contribute to impulsiveness, risk-taking, and emotional intensity during the teen years.

**Q: What is puberty?**
A: The period of sexual maturation during which a person becomes capable of reproducing. The sequence of pubertal changes is universal, though timing varies.

**Q: What are imaginary audience and personal fable?**
A: Forms of adolescent egocentrism. Imaginary audience is the belief that others are constantly watching and judging you. Personal fable is a sense of personal uniqueness and invulnerability.

**Q: What are Kohlberg's three levels of moral reasoning?**
A: Preconventional morality (before age 9) — focused on self-interest and avoiding punishment. Conventional morality (early adolescence) — upholding laws/rules for social approval. Postconventional morality (adolescence and beyond) — reasoning from self-defined ethical principles and basic rights.

**Q: What is moral intuition?**
A: Quick, automatic gut feelings about right and wrong that occur before, or independent of, conscious moral reasoning — though they can be overridden by deliberate thought.

**Q: According to Erikson, what is the adolescent's key task?**
A: Resolving identity vs. role confusion — testing and blending various roles to form a clear, stable sense of self (identity), including social identity, the part of self-concept drawn from group memberships.

**Q: What is the selection effect?**
A: The tendency for people — including teens — to choose companions and environments that already match and reinforce their own traits and interests.

## Adulthood & Aging

**Q: What are the three broad periods of adulthood?**
A: Early adulthood (roughly 20s–30s), middle adulthood (30–65), and late adulthood (65+) — though people vary widely in physical, cognitive, and social development within each.

**Q: What physical changes mark middle and late adulthood?**
A: Middle adulthood brings gradual fertility decline and menopause (women); men experience gradual declines in testosterone and sperm count. Late adulthood brings vision changes, gradual loss of brain cells (affecting memory and complex problem-solving), and changes in smell, hearing, and distance perception.

**Q: What is the social clock?**
A: The culturally preferred timing for major life events, such as marriage, parenthood, and retirement.

**Q: How does cognition change across adulthood?**
A: Early adulthood is a peak period for learning and memory. Middle adulthood shows a greater decline in recall than in recognition. Late adulthood is associated with better retention of meaningful information, though word-production time slows.

**Q: What is terminal decline?**
A: A marked, noticeable drop in cognitive functioning that tends to occur in the last few years before death — distinct from the more gradual cognitive changes of typical aging.

**Q: According to Erikson, what are the key psychosocial tasks of adulthood?**
A: Intimacy vs. isolation (young adulthood — forming close relationships), generativity vs. stagnation (middle adulthood — contributing to the world through family/work), and integrity vs. despair (late adulthood — reflecting on life with a sense of satisfaction or regret).

## Death & Dying

**Q: What do we know about grief from research?**
A: Grief varies by culture and individual, and tends to be more severe when a death is sudden and off the expected social clock. Contrary to popular belief, immediately and forcefully expressing grief doesn't necessarily speed recovery, and grief-stricken people don't move through identical, universal stages.

## Quick Connects

**Q: How do nature and nurture jointly explain attachment style?**
A: Temperament (nature — e.g., "difficult" vs. "easy" babies) interacts with caregiver responsiveness (nurture) to shape attachment security, which Ainsworth's research and the Harlow studies both help demonstrate.

**Q: How does the frontal lobe's slow maturation connect Chapter 2 (brain structures) to adolescent behavior in this chapter?**
A: Since the frontal lobes govern judgment, planning, and impulse control, and don't finish maturing until the mid-20s, the lag between frontal lobe and limbic system development helps explain why adolescents are prone to risk-taking and emotional intensity even as their abstract reasoning (formal operational thinking) matures.
