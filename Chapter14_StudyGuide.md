# Chapter 14 Study Guide: Therapy

*Myers, D. G., & DeWall, C. N. (2023). Psychology in Everyday Life (6th ed.). Worth Publishers.*

## Part 1: Introduction to Treatment

**Q: How do psychotherapy and the biomedical therapies differ?**
A: **Psychotherapy** is a treatment in which a trained therapist uses psychological techniques (exploring early relationships, changing thinking patterns, replacing old behaviors) to help a client overcome difficulties and grow. **Biomedical therapy** treats disorders through biological means — medication, electroconvulsive therapy, or brain stimulation. The two are often combined; many people receive both psychotherapy and medication for the same disorder.

**Q: What are the major categories of psychotherapy?**
A: Most psychotherapies are built on one or more of psychology's major theoretical traditions: **psychodynamic**, **humanistic**, **behavioral**, and **cognitive**. Many therapists today describe their approach as **eclectic** or **integrative**, blending techniques from more than one tradition.

---

## Part 2: Psychoanalysis and Psychodynamic Therapy

**Q: What are the goals and techniques of psychoanalysis?**
A: Freud's **psychoanalysis** aimed to help people achieve healthier, less anxious living by bringing repressed unconscious conflicts into conscious awareness, producing insight. Key techniques:
- **Free association** — saying aloud whatever comes to mind, however trivial or embarrassing.
- **Resistance** — blocks in free association (pauses, topic changes, blanking out) that signal anxiety-laden material.
- **Interpretation** — the analyst's noting of the meaning behind dreams, resistances, and other significant behavior.
- **Transference** — the patient's transfer onto the analyst of emotions tied to other relationships (e.g., a parent).

**Q: How has psychodynamic therapy adapted Freud's ideas?**
A: **Psychodynamic therapists** still focus on important relationships and unconscious influences, including childhood experience, but sessions are typically weekly (not several times a week), last weeks or months (not years), and may be combined with medication — a much briefer, more practical approach than traditional psychoanalysis, which has little scientific support for its underlying theory.

---

## Part 3: Humanistic Therapy

**Q: What are the basic themes of humanistic therapy?**
A: Humanistic therapies emphasize (1) **growth** in self-awareness and self-acceptance over "curing an illness" (hence "clients," not "patients"), (2) **the present and future** over digging into the past, and (3) **conscious thought** over unconscious conflict. Because they share a focus on self-understanding with psychodynamic therapy, both are called **insight therapies**.

**Q: What are the goals and techniques of Carl Rogers' person-centered therapy?**
A: **Person-centered therapy** is nondirective — the client leads the discussion while the therapist listens without judging or interpreting. Rogers believed people already possess the resources for growth, and encouraged therapists to foster it through **acceptance**, **genuineness**, and **empathy**. A core technique is **active listening**: echoing, restating, and clarifying what the client expresses, and acknowledging their feelings. **Unconditional positive regard** — a caring, nonjudgmental, accepting attitude — helps clients feel free to accept even their worst traits and still feel valued.

## Part 4: Behavior Therapies

**Q: How does behavior therapy's basic assumption differ from psychodynamic and humanistic therapy?**
A: Insight therapies assume self-awareness and well-being go together. **Behavior therapy** instead assumes the problem behavior itself *is* the problem — you can understand why you're anxious during exams and still be anxious. Behavior therapists apply learning principles directly to replace unwanted behaviors with constructive ones.

**Q: What classical conditioning techniques are used in exposure therapies and aversive conditioning?**
A: Both are forms of **counterconditioning** — using classical conditioning to evoke a new response to a trigger stimulus.
- **Exposure therapies** repeatedly expose a person to a feared stimulus so the fear response fades. **Systematic desensitization** pairs a relaxation response with a gradually escalating hierarchy of anxiety-triggering situations (a person cannot be relaxed and anxious at the same time). **Virtual reality exposure therapy** uses simulated environments to safely recreate feared situations (flying, heights, social settings).
- **Aversive conditioning** pairs an unwanted behavior (e.g., drinking, nail-biting) with an unpleasant stimulus (nausea-inducing drug, bad-tasting polish), creating a negative association; effects tend to fade once booster sessions stop, since people know the aversive pairing was artificial.

**Q: What is the basic idea of behavior therapies based on operant conditioning?**
A: Because consequences shape voluntary behavior, therapists use **behavior modification** — reinforcing desired behaviors and withholding reinforcement (or applying mild punishment) for undesired ones. A **token economy** rewards desired behaviors (getting up, dressing, cooperating) with tokens exchangeable for privileges; it has been widely used in institutional settings and with children who have ADHD or autism spectrum disorder.

## Part 5: Cognitive Therapies

**Q: What are the goals and techniques of the cognitive therapies?**
A: Cognitive therapies assume our **thinking colors our feelings** — between an event and our emotional response lies interpretation. **Aaron Beck's cognitive therapy** gently questions clients to reveal irrational, self-defeating thoughts (**catastrophizing**) and helps them adopt healthier interpretations. Techniques include questioning beliefs, ranking the intensity of thoughts/emotions, examining likely consequences, **decatastrophizing** (working through the actual worst case, which is often not as bad as imagined), and practicing new, less extreme ways of thinking.

**Q: What is cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), and what are its major variations?**
A: **CBT** combines changing self-defeating thinking (cognitive) with practicing new behaviors in daily life (behavioral); it's widely used for depression, anxiety, OCD, PTSD, and substance use disorders. Two notable variations:
- **Dialectical behavior therapy (DBT)** — developed for chronic suicidal/self-harm behavior; balances acceptance and change, combining individual therapy, group skills training, and mindfulness.
- **Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)** — helps clients accept difficult feelings while committing to actions aligned with their values; effective for depression, anxiety, and chronic pain.

## Part 6: Group, Couple, and Family Therapies

**Q: What are the aims and benefits of group therapy?**
A: **Group therapy** saves time and money, offers a social laboratory for practicing new social behaviors, lets clients see that others share their problems, and provides feedback as people try out new ways of behaving — all while offering less individual therapist attention than one-on-one therapy.

**Q: What is family therapy?**
A: **Family therapy** treats the family as an interconnected system in which each person's actions trigger reactions from others; rather than viewing one member as "the problem," it works to build healthier, more supportive family interactions — helpful when problems stem from relationships (couples therapy is a related, two-person version).

## Part 7: Evaluating Psychotherapies

**Q: Does psychotherapy work, and how can we know?**
A: Client and clinician impressions are unreliable on their own — clients may credit therapy for improvement that would have happened anyway (**regression toward the average**, since crises tend to pass), may be swayed by the **placebo effect** of believing treatment will work, or may rationalize their investment (**effort justification**); clinicians rarely hear from clients who quietly relapse or switch therapists. **Randomized clinical trials** — comparing treated clients to a waiting-list control group, assessed by evaluators unaware of who received therapy — provide the strongest evidence. Statistical digests of hundreds of such trials consistently show that people who receive psychotherapy improve more, and more quickly, than those who do not.

**Q: Are some psychotherapies more effective than others for specific disorders?**
A: No single therapy is best overall, and outcomes don't depend much on the clinician's specific credentials or experience — but *specific* problems respond best to *matched* treatments:
- **Cognitive/CBT** — anxiety, PTSD, insomnia, depression
- **Behavioral conditioning therapies** — phobias, compulsions, bed-wetting, sexual dysfunctions
- **Psychodynamic therapy** — depression and anxiety
- **Person-centered counseling** — mild to moderate depression

Approaches with no scientific support (energy therapies, rebirthing therapies, facilitated communication) and harmful ones (conversion therapy aimed at changing sexual orientation) should be avoided. **Evidence-based practice** integrates research evidence, clinical expertise, and knowledge of the client to guide treatment decisions.

**Q: What three elements are shared by all forms of effective psychotherapy?**
A: (1) **Hope** for discouraged people, (2) a **new perspective** on their symptoms, and (3) an **empathic, trusting, caring relationship** — the **therapeutic alliance**. These common factors help explain why even briefly trained paraprofessionals can help many people effectively.

## Part 8: Human Diversity, Seeking Therapy, and Ethics

**Q: What personal factors influence the client-therapist relationship?**
A: Mismatches can arise from cultural differences — for example, therapists trained in **individualist** traditions (prioritizing personal identity) working with clients from **collectivist** cultures (prioritizing family/social harmony) — as well as differences in religion, and LGBTQ-affirming attitudes. **Cultural competence** — understanding and respecting a client's values, beliefs, and traditions — improves outcomes and trust.

**Q: When should someone seek therapy, and what should they look for?**
A: APA trouble signals include hopelessness, deep/lasting depression, self-destructive behavior, disruptive fears, sudden mood shifts, suicidal thoughts, compulsive rituals, sexual difficulties, and hallucinations. When choosing a therapist, people can meet with more than one, ask about approach/credentials/fees, and weigh their own comfort — the **therapeutic alliance** matters most.

**Q: What ethical principles guide psychotherapy?**
A: APA principles require therapists to benefit clients and do no harm, build trust, act honestly and accurately, promote fairness and access to care, and respect client dignity, privacy, confidentiality, and self-determination.

## Part 9: The Biomedical Therapies

**Q: How do double-blind studies help evaluate drug effectiveness?**
A: In a **double-blind technique**, neither participants nor staff know who received the actual drug versus a placebo, controlling for the **placebo effect** (positive expectations alone producing improvement) so researchers can isolate the drug's true effect.

**Q: What are the four major categories of drug therapy?**
A:
- **Antipsychotic drugs** — treat schizophrenia's positive symptoms (hallucinations, paranoia) largely by blocking dopamine receptors; supports the dopamine hypothesis. Side effects can include sluggishness, tremors, and (with long-term use) **tardive dyskinesia** (involuntary facial/limb movements).
- **Antianxiety drugs** (e.g., Xanax, Ativan) — depress CNS activity to calm anxiety; can be addictive, with withdrawal symptoms upon stopping.
- **Antidepressant drugs** — increase availability of norepinephrine or serotonin. **SSRIs** (Prozac, Zoloft, Paxil) block serotonin reuptake at the synapse; full effect can take about 4 weeks. Fast-acting alternatives like **ketamine** can relieve depression within hours, though effects often fade within about a week.
- **Mood-stabilizing drugs** (Depakote, **lithium**) — control the manic (and often depressive) episodes of bipolar disorders; lithium benefits roughly 7 in 10 long-term users and substantially reduces suicide risk in that group.

**Q: How are brain stimulation and psychosurgery used to treat specific disorders?**
A:
- **Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)** — a brief electric current triggers a 30–60 second brain seizure in an anesthetized patient; highly effective for severe, treatment-resistant depression, though its mechanism remains unclear and relapse is common without follow-up treatment.
- **Transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS)** — a weak scalp current; modestly effective for depression.
- **Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)** — magnetic pulses to the skull over several sessions; helps 30–40% of people with depression and some schizophrenia symptoms, with fewer side effects than ECT.
- **Deep brain stimulation (DBS)** — implanted electrodes activated by a pacemaker inhibit overactive brain areas linked to depression; used for cases resistant to drugs and ECT.
- **Psychosurgery** — removes or destroys brain tissue; irreversible and rarely used today. The **lobotomy**, developed by Egas Moniz, cut connections between the frontal lobes and emotional centers; often left patients permanently impaired and was abandoned once calming drugs became available. Modern micropsychosurgery is used only as a last resort.

## Part 10: Preventing Disorders and Building Resilience

**Q: What is preventive mental health, and why does it matter?**
A: **Preventive mental health** is "upstream" work — addressing the social conditions (poverty, discrimination, lack of meaningful work, chronic stress) that produce psychological casualties, rather than only treating people after problems develop. **Community psychologists** focus on shaping environments that support psychological health and empower people.

**Q: What is resilience, and what is posttraumatic growth?**
A: **Resilience** is the personal strength that allows most people to cope with stress and recover from adversity or trauma without lasting harm — the large majority of people exposed to disasters, combat, or serious injury do not develop PTSD. **Posttraumatic growth** describes positive psychological change that can follow struggling with crisis or trauma, including greater appreciation for life, deeper relationships, and increased personal strength.
