# Chapter 5: Sensation & Perception — Study Guide

---

## Basic Concepts

**Q: What is the difference between sensation and perception?**
A: Sensation is the process by which sensory receptors detect and receive stimulus energy from the environment. Perception is how the brain organizes and interprets that sensory input into something meaningful. You can have normal sensation but impaired perception (as with prosopagnosia/face blindness).

**Q: What is transduction and what are its three steps?**
A: Transduction is the process of converting outside energy into neural impulses the brain can use. The three steps are: (1) receive sensory stimulation, often using specialized receptor cells; (2) transform that stimulation into neural impulses; (3) deliver the neural information to the brain.

**Q: What is bottom-up processing?**
A: Processing that starts with raw sensory data — detecting lines, angles, colors, sounds — and builds upward toward recognition and meaning. Driven by the stimulus itself.

**Q: What is top-down processing?**
A: Processing guided by experience, expectations, and prior knowledge. The brain uses what it already knows to interpret what the senses detect. Can cause us to perceive what we expect rather than what's there.

**Q: What is the absolute threshold?**
A: The minimum stimulus intensity needed to detect a stimulus 50% of the time. Stimuli below this level are subliminal.

**Q: Can subliminal stimuli influence us?**
A: People can be primed by subliminal stimuli, but research does not support powerful or lasting persuasive effects on behavior.

**Q: What is the difference threshold (jnd)?**
A: The minimum difference between two stimuli that a person can detect 50% of the time. Also called the just noticeable difference.

**Q: What does Weber's Law state?**
A: Two stimuli must differ by a constant minimum *percentage* (not a fixed amount) to be noticed. The percentage varies by sense — for example, two lights must differ by about 8% in intensity, two weights by about 2%.

**Q: What is sensory adaptation and why is it useful?**
A: Reduced sensitivity to a constant, unchanging stimulus over time (nerve cells fire less frequently). It's useful because it frees our attention to focus on novel or changing stimuli rather than constant background input.

**Q: What is a perceptual set?**
A: A mental predisposition — shaped by experience and expectations — that influences what we perceive. It can cause us to see, hear, or feel what we expect rather than what's actually there.

**Q: How do context, motivation, and emotion affect perception?**
A: Context creates expectations that shape interpretation (e.g., the same shape read as a letter or number depending on surrounding context). Motivation can make desirable objects seem closer. Emotions can push ambiguous stimuli toward mood-consistent interpretations (e.g., sad music makes "mourning" more likely than "morning").

## Vision

**Q: What physical properties of light determine what we see?**
A: Wavelength determines hue (color) — short waves appear blue-violet, long waves appear red. Amplitude (intensity) determines brightness.

**Q: What is the path light takes from the eye to the brain?**
A: Light → cornea → pupil (controlled by iris) → lens (focuses image) → retina (rods and cones) → bipolar cells → ganglion cells → optic nerve → thalamus → visual cortex (occipital lobe).

**Q: What is the difference between rods and cones?**
A: Rods (~120 million): located in the periphery of the retina; highly sensitive to dim light; cannot detect color or fine detail. Cones (~6 million): clustered in the fovea; detect color and fine detail; require more light.

**Q: What is the blind spot?**
A: The point where the optic nerve exits the eye. It has no receptor cells and therefore cannot detect light.

**Q: What is the fovea?**
A: The central area of the retina where cones are most densely packed. It produces our sharpest, most detailed vision.

**Q: What do the Young-Helmholtz trichromatic theory and opponent-process theory each explain?**
A: Trichromatic theory: the retina has three types of color receptors (red, green, blue) whose combined responses produce all colors. Opponent-process theory: color is processed in opposing pairs (red-green, blue-yellow, black-white) further up in the visual pathway. Together they explain both color mixing and afterimages.

**Q: What is prosopagnosia?**
A: Face blindness — the inability to recognize faces despite normal vision. It illustrates that sensation and perception are separate processes.

**Q: What is the figure-ground principle?**
A: The perceptual tendency to organize stimuli into a figure (the object of focus) standing out from a background (ground).

**Q: What are the Gestalt principles of perceptual organization?**
A: Principles describing how the brain groups sensory elements into wholes: proximity (nearby things grouped together), similarity (similar things grouped together), continuity (we follow smooth lines), and closure (we fill in gaps to complete a whole shape).

**Q: What is depth perception and how do we achieve it?**
A: The ability to perceive objects in three dimensions despite having a two-dimensional retinal image. Achieved through binocular cues (using both eyes) and monocular cues (available to one eye).

**Q: What is retinal disparity?**
A: A binocular depth cue: each eye receives a slightly different image of the world. The greater the disparity, the closer the object.

**Q: Name four monocular depth cues.**
A: Relative size (smaller = farther), interposition (blocked object = farther), linear perspective (parallel lines converge with distance), texture gradient (finer texture = farther away). Also: relative height, relative clarity, light and shadow, relative motion.

**Q: What is perceptual constancy?**
A: The ability to perceive objects as stable and unchanging even as illumination and retinal images change. Types include size, shape, color, and brightness constancy.

**Q: What is color constancy?**
A: Perceiving familiar objects as having consistent color even when the wavelengths of light they reflect change (e.g., a red apple still looks red in dim light).

## Hearing

**Q: What physical properties of sound determine what we hear?**
A: Frequency (measured in hertz) determines pitch — high frequency = high pitch. Amplitude (measured in decibels) determines loudness.

**Q: What is the path sound takes from the ear to the brain?**
A: Sound waves → eardrum vibrates → three middle ear bones (hammer, anvil, stirrup) amplify vibrations → oval window → cochlea (fluid ripples) → basilar membrane bends hair cells → auditory nerve → thalamus → auditory cortex (temporal lobe).

**Q: What are hair cells and why do they matter?**
A: Tiny receptor cells (~16,000) lining the basilar membrane of the cochlea. Bending their cilia — even by the width of an atom — triggers a neural signal. Damage to hair cells is the primary cause of sensorineural hearing loss, and unlike many body cells, they do not regenerate.

**Q: What is the difference between place theory and frequency theory of pitch?**
A: Place theory: different pitches activate hair cells at different locations along the basilar membrane (better explains high-frequency sounds). Frequency theory: the rate of nerve impulses matches the frequency of the tone (better explains low-frequency sounds). Both work together.

**Q: What is sensorineural hearing loss vs. conduction hearing loss?**
A: Sensorineural (nerve deafness): damage to the cochlea's hair cells or auditory nerves — most common, irreversible. Conduction: damage to the mechanical system (eardrum or middle ear bones) that conducts sound to the cochlea — less common.

**Q: What is a cochlear implant?**
A: A device that translates sounds into electrical signals delivered directly to the cochlea's nerves, bypassing damaged hair cells. Most effective when implanted early in life.

**Q: How does the brain locate sounds?**
A: By detecting that sound waves reach one ear slightly sooner and at greater intensity than the other. The brain calculates location from these tiny differences (as small as 0.000027 seconds).

**Q: At what decibel level does hearing damage risk begin?**
A: Prolonged exposure above 85 decibels can cause hearing loss. Normal conversation is about 60 dB; a jet plane at 500 feet is about 110 dB.

## Touch & Pain

**Q: What are the four basic skin senses?**
A: Pressure, warmth, cold, and pain. Other sensations (tickle, itch, wetness) are combinations of these.

**Q: Why is pain described as a biopsychosocial event?**
A: Because it is shaped by biological factors (nociceptors, genes, gate-control mechanisms), psychological factors (attention, memory, expectations, fear), and social-cultural factors (culture, social context, others' pain behavior).

**Q: What are nociceptors?**
A: Sensory receptors — mostly in the skin — that detect hurtful temperatures, pressure, or chemicals and send pain signals to the brain.

**Q: What is gate-control theory?**
A: The idea that the spinal cord contains a neural "gate" that can block or allow pain signals to reach the brain. Small nerve fibers open the gate; large-fiber activity (from massage, acupuncture, or electrical stimulation) can close it.

**Q: What is phantom limb sensation?**
A: Pain or sensation felt in a limb that no longer exists. Demonstrates that pain is created by the brain, not just peripheral nerves — the brain misinterprets other neural activity in the absence of normal input.

**Q: What is the peak-end rule in pain memory?**
A: People remember pain based on its peak intensity and how it felt at the end — not how long it lasted. A procedure that ends with milder pain may be remembered as less painful even if it lasted longer overall.

**Q: How do placebos, distraction, and hypnosis each reduce pain?**
A: Placebos: belief in the treatment triggers endorphin release, dampening CNS pain response. Distraction: redirects attention away from pain, activating brain pathways that reduce pain signals. Hypnosis: inhibits pain-related brain activity and blocks attention to painful stimuli; most effective when combined with placebo and distraction.

**Q: What are endorphins?**
A: The brain's natural painkillers. Released in response to severe pain or vigorous exercise. Opioid drugs mimic endorphins; chronic opioid use can suppress the body's natural endorphin production.

## Taste & Smell

**Q: What are the five basic tastes and what does each signal?**
A: Sweet (energy source), salty (sodium), sour (potential toxic acid), bitter (potential poisons), umami (proteins for growth and repair).

**Q: How do taste buds work?**
A: Each bump on the tongue contains 200+ taste buds. Each bud has 50–100 receptor cells that catch food molecules and release neurotransmitters to the brain's temporal lobe. Taste buds regenerate every 1–2 weeks.

**Q: How does smell (olfaction) differ from other senses in its brain pathway?**
A: Olfactory neurons bypass the thalamus and connect more directly to the limbic system (memory and emotion areas). This is why smells are so strongly tied to memories and emotional responses.

**Q: How many odors can humans detect?**
A: An estimated 1 trillion, produced by different combinations of signals from hundreds of receptor types.

**Q: What are pheromones?**
A: Chemical signals secreted by members of a species that can influence the behavior or physiology of other members. May play a role in sexual attraction and social behavior.

**Q: Where are taste and smell processed in the brain, and why does that matter?**
A: Both are processed near the border of the frontal and temporal lobes, and smell connects directly to memory/emotion areas. This proximity explains why smells powerfully trigger taste memories, and vice versa.

## Body Senses

**Q: What is kinesthesia?**
A: The sense of the position and movement of individual body parts. Relies on proprioceptors — sensors in muscles, tendons, and joints — and is processed in the cerebellum.

**Q: What are proprioceptors?**
A: Millions of position and motion sensors in muscles, tendons, and joints throughout the body that send constant feedback to the brain, enabling kinesthesia.

**Q: What is the vestibular sense?**
A: The sense of body movement, position, and balance. Uses hair-like receptors in the semicircular canals and vestibular sacs of the inner ear; processed in the cerebellum.

**Q: What are the semicircular canals?**
A: Three fluid-filled loops in the inner ear arranged in three planes. When the head rotates, fluid shifts and bends receptor cells, sending balance information to the cerebellum.

**Q: What is sensory interaction?**
A: The principle that one sense can influence another — for example, smell enhancing taste, or vision affecting balance. The brain integrates input from multiple senses simultaneously.

**Q: What is embodied cognition?**
A: The influence of bodily sensations and physical states on cognitive judgments and preferences — for example, holding a warm cup making someone feel warmer toward another person.

**Q: What is synesthesia?**
A: A condition in which stimulation of one sense automatically triggers an experience in another (e.g., seeing colors when hearing music). Occurs when brain circuits for two senses become joined.

## ESP

**Q: What is extrasensory perception (ESP) and what forms does it take?**
A: The claim that perception can occur without sensory input. Includes telepathy (mind-to-mind communication), clairvoyance (perceiving remote events), and precognition (perceiving future events).

**Q: What is the scientific consensus on ESP?**
A: Most research psychologists are skeptical. Controlled, reproducible evidence does not support ESP, and studies claiming to support it have been widely criticized for methodological flaws.

## Quick Connects

**Q: How are sensation and perception related to bottom-up and top-down processing?**
A: Sensation feeds bottom-up processing — raw data coming in from the senses. Perception involves top-down processing — the brain using prior knowledge and expectations to interpret that data. Both happen simultaneously.

**Q: How does perceptual set relate to top-down processing?**
A: A perceptual set is a specific form of top-down processing — the mental framework of expectations we bring to a situation shapes what we perceive before the sensory data is even fully processed.

**Q: How does sensory adaptation relate to the absolute threshold?**
A: The absolute threshold is the minimum level needed to detect a stimulus. Sensory adaptation raises the effective threshold for constant stimuli — after prolonged exposure, you need a stronger or different signal to notice a change.
