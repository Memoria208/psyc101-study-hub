## Part 1: Stress — Basic Concepts

**Q: What is stress, and how do psychologists distinguish it from a stressor and a stress reaction?**
A: Stress is the process of appraising an event as threatening or challenging, and responding to it. The stressor is the challenge or event itself; the stress reaction is the resulting physical and emotional response. Appraisal happens in two steps: a primary appraisal (is this a stressor?) and a secondary appraisal (can I handle it?).

**Q: Why can the same event (like an exam) produce either a challenge response or a threat response?**
A: Events flow through a psychological filter. If we feel prepared and capable, we may appraise the event as a challenge, becoming aroused and focused in a way that helps performance. If we feel unprepared, we appraise it as a threat, producing distress instead.

**Q: What are the three main categories of stressors?**
A: Catastrophes (large-scale, unpredictable disasters such as earthquakes, hurricanes, or pandemics), significant life changes (graduating, marrying, divorcing, losing a job), and daily hassles (lost keys, notifications, chronic workplace demands). All three can increase disease and mortality risk.

**Q: What are the three types of motivational conflict that contribute to everyday stress?**
A: Approach–approach (choosing between two attractive options, least stressful), avoidance–avoidance (choosing between two unattractive options), and approach–avoidance (a single goal with both appealing and unappealing features, producing simultaneous attraction and repulsion).

## Part 2: Stress Reactions — From Alarm to Exhaustion

**Q: What did Walter Cannon discover about the body's stress response?**
A: Cannon found that extreme cold, lack of oxygen, and emotionally arousing events all trigger stress hormones from the adrenal glands. The sympathetic nervous system responds by increasing heart rate and respiration, diverting blood to skeletal muscles, dulling pain, and releasing stored energy — the fight-or-flight response.

**Q: How did Hans Selye extend Cannon's findings, and what is the general adaptation syndrome (GAS)?**
A: Selye showed the body's stress response is general — the same "alarm" sounds no matter the specific stressor. He described this as a three-phase general adaptation syndrome: (1) alarm reaction — the sympathetic nervous system activates suddenly; (2) resistance — the body draws on reserves while staying physiologically aroused; (3) exhaustion — reserves are depleted, increasing vulnerability to illness. Extreme or constant stress is also linked to shorter telomeres.

**Q: Besides fight-or-flight, what other stress response patterns exist?**
A: Withdrawal (disengaging from the stressor) and the tend-and-befriend response, in which — especially among females — stress prompts people to seek and give social support rather than fight or flee.

## Part 3: Stress Effects and Health

**Q: What is psychoneuroimmunology, and how are the mind and immune system connected?**
A: Psychoneuroimmunology is the study of how psychological, neural, and endocrine processes interact to affect the immune system and health. Emotions affect the brain, which controls endocrine hormones, which in turn influence immune functioning.

**Q: What are the four main immune-system cell types, and what do they do?**
A: B lymphocytes form in the bone marrow and release antibodies against bacterial infections. T lymphocytes form in the thymus and attack cancer cells, viruses, and foreign tissue. Macrophage cells identify and ingest invaders and worn-out cells. Natural killer cells attack cancerous and virus-infected cells.

**Q: How can the immune system become dysfunctional under stress?**
A: It can overreact, attacking the body's own tissue (contributing to arthritis or allergic/autoimmune reactions), or underreact, allowing infections to flare, dormant viruses to reactivate, or cancer cells to multiply. Stressed people also heal from wounds more slowly and catch colds more readily.

**Q: How does personality relate to coronary heart disease risk?**
A: In Friedman and Rosenman's classic studies, Type A people (competitive, hard-driving, impatient, anger-prone) were far more likely to suffer heart attacks than easygoing Type B people — in one study, 69% of heart attacks occurred among Type A men, and no "pure" Type B suffered one. Later research pinpointed anger and hostility, not just time pressure, as the toxic core: anger diverts blood flow away from organs like the liver, letting cholesterol and fat build up around the heart.

**Q: How do pessimism and depression relate to heart disease?**
A: Higher pessimism is linked to more than double the risk of developing heart disease compared with optimists. Depression is also a serious risk factor — depressed people tend to age faster, die sooner, and are more likely to develop further heart problems after an initial heart attack.

## Part 4: Coping With Stress

**Q: What is the difference between problem-focused and emotion-focused coping?**
A: Problem-focused coping tries to change the stressor directly or change how we interact with it — used when we feel some control over the situation. Emotion-focused coping tries to relieve the emotional impact of a stressor we feel we cannot change, for example by seeking comfort from friends; some emotion-focused strategies help (gaining distance, using hobbies) while others can harm health (using comfort food to cope).

**Q: What is personal control, and how does a lack of control affect health?**
A: Personal control is one's sense of controlling the environment rather than feeling helpless. Repeated exposure to uncontrollable events can produce learned helplessness — passive resignation and hopelessness — which raises stress hormones, blood pressure, and disease risk. Studies of nursing home residents and workers show that increasing perceived control (more choices, more say) improves alertness, morale, and engagement.

**Q: What is the "tyranny of choice," and how does it relate to personal control?**
A: While some control is better than none, an excess of choice can backfire, producing information overload, decreased satisfaction, increased depression, or behavioral paralysis (e.g., people were less satisfied choosing from 30 jam options than from 6).

**Q: What is the difference between an internal and an external locus of control?**
A: People with an internal locus of control believe they control their own fate; those with an external locus of control believe chance or outside forces control it. Internals tend to achieve more, act more independently, enjoy better health, and feel less depressed than externals.

**Q: How do optimism and pessimism affect health, and what is the danger of excessive optimism?**
A: Optimists tend to cope better with stress, have stronger immune responses, and in several long-term studies show lower mortality risk than pessimists. However, excessive or "unrealistic" optimism can blind people to real risks, leading them to underestimate personal danger (e.g., underestimating their chances of illness or overestimating their ability to resist temptation).

**Q: What are the three main emotion regulation strategies described by James Gross, and how do their consequences differ?**
A: (1) Situation selection — changing your situation to influence your feelings — is linked to greater well-being and fewer negative emotions. (2) Cognitive reappraisal — changing how you think about a situation — is linked to better social relationships and coping with stress. (3) Suppression — hiding or dampening emotional expression — tends to produce less authentic social experiences, poorer coping, and more negative emotion. The first two strategies are generally more helpful than the third.

**Q: How does social support benefit health, and how does finding meaning help people cope?**
A: Social support — feeling liked, encouraged, and helped by others — calms the cardiovascular system, lowers blood pressure and stress hormones, and strengthens immune functioning. Finding meaning in suffering (through close relationships, or talking/writing about a difficult experience) helps people cope with catastrophes and major life changes.

## Part 5: Managing Stress Effects

**Q: How does aerobic exercise help manage stress?**
A: Sustained aerobic activity increases heart and lung fitness, boosts energy, confidence, and mood, and strengthens relationships. It reduces depression and anxiety partly by increasing arousal and serotonin activity, and it enhances both the quality and quantity of life.

**Q: What are the benefits of relaxation and meditation, and what is mindfulness meditation specifically?**
A: Relaxation techniques improve well-being and relieve headaches, high blood pressure, anxiety, and insomnia, and can even speed wound healing. Meditation — used across world religions for centuries — reduces suffering and increases awareness, insight, and compassion. Mindfulness meditation, a nonjudgmental, accepting attention to present experience, boosts happiness, reduces anxiety and depression, and is linked to better sleep and immune functioning.

**Q: What three brain changes help explain mindfulness's effects?**
A: Mindfulness (1) strengthens connections among brain regions involved in attention and reflection, (2) activates brain regions associated with emotion regulation (more prefrontal cortex activity, less amygdala activity), and (3) calms brain activation in emotionally charged situations.

**Q: What is the "faith factor," and what explains the correlation between religious involvement and health?**
A: The faith factor is the well-documented correlation between religious involvement and longer, healthier life, even after controlling for age, sex, and other health risks. Three explanatory factors are healthy behaviors (lower smoking/drinking rates), social support (a built-in support network), and positive emotions (a stable worldview and sense of hope). Because this is correlational, it cannot prove religion itself causes better health.

## Part 6: Happiness and Well-Being

**Q: How do psychologists define happiness and subjective well-being?**
A: Happiness is having more positive than negative feelings — an enduring prevalence of positive emotion, not simply the absence of problems. Subjective well-being is a person's own self-perceived satisfaction with life, which researchers use as a key measure of flourishing.

**Q: What is the feel-good, do-good phenomenon?**
A: People are more likely to be helpful toward others when they are already in a good mood.

**Q: What is the adaptation-level phenomenon, and how does it relate to happiness?**
A: We judge new stimuli — sounds, lights, income — relative to a neutral level defined by our own past experience. This helps explain why a raise or a new possession brings only temporary happiness before we adapt to the new baseline.

**Q: What is relative deprivation, and how does it affect life satisfaction?**
A: Relative deprivation is the perception of being worse off compared with those we use as a reference point. Even when objective circumstances are similar, people who compare themselves with wealthier or more successful others often report lower life satisfaction.

**Q: What factors help explain individual differences in happiness?**
A: Genes contribute meaningfully — twin studies estimate about 36% of the variation in happiness ratings is heritable — and each person's happiness tends to fluctuate around a personal set point. Personal history, culture (individualist vs. communal values), and life circumstances also shape happiness.

**Q: Does money buy happiness?**
A: Personal income predicts happiness mainly up to the point of meeting basic needs, safety, and occasional special treats; beyond that, additional wealth adds comparatively little. Extreme poverty, however, is strongly linked to misery, and rising income inequality within a country predicts lower national happiness.

**Q: Do bad events cause long-term unhappiness, and is happiness really independent of other people?**
A: Even very stressful events (such as 9/11 or the COVID-19 pandemic) usually cause only a temporary dip in happiness, since most people show resilience and recover over time — though grief and serious trauma can linger longer. And happiness is not independent of others: people tend to perceive others' lives as more active or successful than their own, and social comparison (relative deprivation) measurably affects life satisfaction, especially for those with lower incomes.

**Q: What are some evidence-based strategies for a happier life?**
A: Taking control of your time, acting happy, seeking work and leisure that use your skills, buying experiences rather than things, prioritizing sleep and exercise, giving priority to close relationships, practicing gratitude, challenging negative thinking, and nurturing a sense of meaning or spirituality.
