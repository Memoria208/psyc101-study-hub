# Chapter 11 Study Guide — Social Psychology

*Myers & DeWall, Psychology in Everyday Life, 6th ed.*

---

### What Is Social Psychology's Focus?

**Q: What are the three main focuses of social psychology?**
A: How we think about, influence, and relate to one another. Personality psychologists study why *different* people act differently in the *same* situation; social psychologists study why the *same* person acts differently in *different* situations.

---

### Social Thinking

**Q: How does the fundamental attribution error describe how we tend to explain others' behavior compared with our own?**
A: We tend to overestimate the influence of a stranger's personality traits and underestimate the power of their situation. We're less likely to make this error about ourselves or people we know well, because we've seen them across many different contexts.

**Q: What is an attitude, and how do attitudes and actions affect each other?**
A: An attitude is a feeling, often based on beliefs, that predisposes us to respond in a certain way. The relationship is two-way: attitudes shape actions, and actions shape attitudes (the foot-in-the-door effect, role-playing, and cognitive dissonance theory all show behavior changing belief).

**Q: How do peripheral route persuasion and central route persuasion differ?**
A: Peripheral route persuasion uses attention-getting cues (attractiveness, emotion) for quick, superficial judgments. Central route persuasion uses evidence and argument to engage careful thought — it's slower to build but more durable.

### Social Influence

**Q: How does culture affect our behavior?**
A: Culture — the behaviors, ideas, and values passed down through a group — shapes everything from individualism vs. collectivism to how tightly or loosely social norms are enforced, and it can change faster than genetics ever could.

**Q: What do experiments on conformity and obedience reveal about the power of social influence?**
A: Asch's line experiments showed that people will conform to an obviously wrong group answer roughly a third of the time. Milgram's obedience experiments showed that roughly 60–65% of ordinary people will obey a legitimate authority even to the point of apparently harming another person — obedience was highest when the authority was close, legitimate, and backed by a prestigious institution, and when the victim was distant and there were no models of defiance.

**Q: What do the social influence studies teach us about the power of the individual?**
A: Social control (the situation) and personal control (the individual) interact. A firm, unwavering minority can sway a majority — minority influence — showing that individuals still matter even amid powerful group pressures.

**Q: How does the presence of others influence our actions, via social facilitation, social loafing, and deindividuation?**
A: Social facilitation: others' presence boosts arousal, which strengthens our dominant (correct-on-easy-tasks, incorrect-on-hard-tasks) response. Social loafing: in group efforts, individuals often exert less effort because they feel less accountable. Deindividuation: anonymity and group arousal can strip away self-awareness and self-restraint, sometimes enabling destructive behavior.

**Q: How can group interaction enable group polarization and groupthink?**
A: Group polarization: discussing shared views with like-minded others strengthens the group's prevailing attitude, whether that attitude is benign or extreme. Groupthink: a group's desire for harmony can override realistic appraisal of alternatives, leading to poor decisions (e.g., the Bay of Pigs invasion).

### Social Relations

**Q: What are the three parts of prejudice? How do explicit and implicit prejudice differ?**
A: Prejudice combines negative emotions, stereotyped beliefs, and a predisposition to discriminate. Explicit prejudice is consciously held and openly expressed; implicit prejudice operates below conscious awareness and shows up in automatic associations or bodily responses even among people who deny bias.

**Q: What are some social, emotional, and cognitive roots of prejudice?**
A: Social inequality breeds justifying attitudes (just-world phenomenon) and ingroup bias. Negative emotions like fear or frustration can trigger scapegoating. Cognitively, we simplify the world through categories, which can produce outgroup homogeneity, the other-race effect, and vivid-case (availability heuristic) stereotyping. Hindsight bias and system justification can further fuel victim blaming.

**Q: What biological, psychological, and social-cultural factors trigger aggression?**
A: Biologically: genetic predispositions, testosterone, and neural circuits that inhibit or facilitate aggression. Psychologically and socially: aversive events (the frustration-aggression principle), reinforcement and modeling, and media/social scripts that normalize violence.

**Q: Why do we befriend or fall in love with some people but not others?**
A: Three key ingredients: proximity (nearness, boosted by the mere exposure effect), physical attractiveness (a strong predictor of initial liking), and similarity (shared attitudes and interests) — all consistent with the reward theory of attraction, which says we like people whose company is rewarding.

**Q: How does romantic love typically change as time passes?**
A: Passionate love — an intense, aroused absorption in another — typically mellows into companionate love, a deeper, calmer attachment supported by oxytocin. Enduring relationships also depend on equity (balanced give-and-take) and self-disclosure (mutual, escalating openness).

**Q: What is altruism? When are we most — and least — likely to help?**
A: Altruism is unselfish concern for others' welfare. Helping requires noticing an incident, interpreting it as an emergency, and assuming responsibility — but the bystander effect shows that the more bystanders present, the less likely any one person is to help. We're more likely to help when we're not in a hurry, in a good mood, feeling guilty, or when the reciprocity norm or social-responsibility norm applies.

**Q: What social processes fuel conflict, and how can we transform prejudice into peace?**
A: Conflict often grows from mirror-image perceptions — each side sees itself as good and the other as hostile — which can become self-fulfilling prophecies. Positive, equal-status contact and especially cooperation toward superordinate goals (shared goals requiring joint effort) have been shown to turn rival groups into allies.
