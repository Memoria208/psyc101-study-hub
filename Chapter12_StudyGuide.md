# Chapter 12 Study Guide — Personality

*Myers & DeWall, Psychology in Everyday Life, 6th ed.*

---

### What Is Personality?

**Q: What is personality, and what theories inform our understanding of it?**
A: Personality is a person's characteristic pattern of thinking, feeling, and acting. Four major perspectives explain it: psychodynamic theories (unconscious motives and childhood conflicts), humanistic theories (inner capacity for growth), trait theories (stable, describable patterns of behavior), and social-cognitive theories (the interaction between traits and social context).

---

### Psychodynamic Theories

**Q: How did Freud's treatment of psychological disorders lead to his view of the unconscious mind?**
A: Freud encountered patients with physical symptoms that had no medical explanation. He concluded the causes were mental, not physical, and that a large unconscious region of the mind hides unacceptable thoughts, wishes, and memories that still influence us. He used free association to trace patients' thoughts back to repressed material.

**Q: What was Freud's view of personality structure?**
A: Personality arises from conflict between impulse and restraint, managed by three systems: the id (unconscious, pleasure-seeking), the ego (conscious "executive," reality-oriented), and the superego (moral conscience). The ego mediates between the id's demands and the superego's restraints.

**Q: What developmental stages did Freud propose?**
A: The psychosexual stages — oral, anal, phallic, latency, and genital — each centered on a different erogenous zone. Freud believed unresolved conflict at any stage (such as the Oedipus complex during the phallic stage) could cause fixation, leaving pleasure-seeking energy locked at that stage into adulthood.

**Q: How did Freud think people defended themselves against anxiety?**
A: Through unconscious defense mechanisms that distort reality to protect the ego — repression (the foundational mechanism), regression, reaction formation, projection, rationalization, displacement, and denial.

**Q: Which of Freud's ideas did his followers accept or reject?**
A: Neo-Freudians (Adler, Horney, Jung) kept the id/ego/superego structure, the importance of the unconscious and childhood, and defense mechanisms — but emphasized the conscious mind more and doubted that sex and aggression were the only major motives, favoring social and status-related motives instead.

**Q: What are projective tests, and what criticisms have they faced?**
A: Projective tests (like the Rorschach inkblot test and the TAT) ask people to respond to ambiguous stimuli, on the assumption that their responses reveal unconscious conflicts. They have shown weak validity (poor prediction) and weak reliability (inconsistent scoring across raters).

**Q: How do today's psychologists view Freud's psychoanalysis?**
A: Most reject the specifics — the idea that development is fixed in childhood, that dreams disguise unconscious wishes, and that repression is common (research shows it's actually rare, even after severe trauma). But they credit Freud for highlighting the unconscious, the irrational, and the tension between biological impulse and social restraint. Modern cognitive research does confirm we have a large unconscious "two-track mind" — just not the same one Freud described.

### Humanistic Theories

**Q: How did humanistic psychologists view personality?**
A: In contrast to Freud's focus on conflict and pathology, Maslow and Rogers emphasized healthy people's striving for self-determination and self-realization, studying people's own reported experiences rather than lab behavior.

**Q: What did Maslow and Rogers each propose?**
A: Maslow proposed a hierarchy of needs, topped by self-actualization — fulfilling one's potential once basic needs are met. Rogers' person-centered perspective held that people grow toward their potential in an environment offering acceptance (unconditional positive regard), genuineness, and empathy. For Rogers, self-concept — the answer to "Who am I?" — is personality's central feature.

**Q: What criticisms have humanistic theories faced?**
A: Critics say the concepts are vague and reflect the theorists' personal values rather than science; that encouraging people to prioritize self-satisfaction can foster self-indulgence; and that the theories underestimate humanity's capacity for evil.

### Trait Theories

**Q: How do psychologists use traits to describe personality?**
A: Traits are stable, enduring behavior patterns identified through statistical clustering of behaviors (factors). Eysenck reduced personality to two dimensions — extraversion/introversion and emotional stability/instability. Today's dominant model is the Big Five: openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism.

**Q: Does research support the consistency of traits over time and across situations?**
A: Traits become more stable with age and reliably predict a person's *average* behavior across many situations, but they predict behavior in any single specific situation only weakly — this is the person-situation controversy. Traits still "leave tracks," showing up in music preferences, writing style, and personal spaces.

### Social-Cognitive Theories

**Q: How do social-cognitive theorists view personality?**
A: Bandura's reciprocal determinism holds that behavior, internal personal factors (like self-efficacy), and the environment all influence one another. People choose environments based on their personality, their personality shapes how they interpret events, and their personality helps create the very situations they react to.

**Q: How do social-cognitive psychologists assess personality?**
A: By observing behavior in realistic situations, on the principle that the best predictor of future behavior is a person's past behavior in similar situations — not a personality test or an interviewer's gut feeling.

### Exploring the Self

**Q: What are possible selves, and what is the spotlight effect?**
A: Possible selves are the visions of who we dream of becoming and who we fear becoming; they motivate specific goals. The spotlight effect is our tendency to overestimate how much others notice and judge us.

**Q: How important is self-esteem to well-being, and what are its costs?**
A: High self-esteem is linked to being outgoing and happier, but research suggests self-esteem is often a byproduct of doing well, not the cause of it — artificially inflating it (e.g., empty praise) doesn't reliably improve performance. Threatened self-esteem can lead people to act negatively toward others, while excessively inflated (narcissistic) self-esteem can cause defensiveness and aggression when criticized.

**Q: What is self-serving bias, and how do defensive and secure self-esteem differ?**
A: Self-serving bias is the tendency to see ourselves favorably — taking credit for success more than failure, and rating ourselves better than average. Defensive self-esteem is fragile and reacts to criticism with anger; secure self-esteem is sturdier, relying less on others' evaluations and allowing genuine self-acceptance.

**Q: How do individualist and collectivist cultures shape values and goals?**
A: Individualist cultures prioritize personal goals and define identity by individual traits; collectivist cultures prioritize group goals and define identity by group belonging. Neither is "better" — they carry different trade-offs (e.g., ease of leaving relationships vs. deeper, more stable ones).
