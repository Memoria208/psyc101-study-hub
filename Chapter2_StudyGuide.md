# Chapter 2: The Biology of Behavior & Consciousness — Study Guide

## Neural Communication

**Q: What is a neuron, and what are its main parts?**
A: A neuron is a nerve cell — the basic building block of the nervous system. The cell body contains the nucleus and is the cell's life-support center. Dendrites receive and integrate incoming information. The axon sends messages out to other neurons, muscles, or glands.

**Q: What happens during an action potential?**
A: When excitatory signals to a neuron exceed inhibitory signals by a certain threshold, the combined signals trigger an action potential — an electrical impulse that travels down the axon. This is an all-or-none response: the neuron either fires completely or not at all.

**Q: What is the refractory period?**
A: A brief resting pause after a neuron fires, lasting a fraction of a second, before it can fire again.

**Q: What happens at the synapse?**
A: The synapse is the junction between the axon tip of a sending neuron and the dendrite or cell body of a receiving neuron. Neurotransmitters cross the tiny synaptic gap to carry the message. Afterward, reuptake reabsorbs the neurotransmitter back into the sending neuron.

**Q: What do glial cells do?**
A: They support, nourish, and protect neurons, provide myelin insulation, and send messages to one another.

**Q: What are the major neurotransmitters and their functions?**
A: Acetylcholine (ACh) — muscle action, learning, memory (deteriorates in Alzheimer's). Dopamine — movement, learning, attention, emotion (oversupply linked to schizophrenia; undersupply to Parkinson's). Serotonin — mood, hunger, sleep, arousal (undersupply linked to depression). Norepinephrine — alertness and arousal. GABA — major inhibitory neurotransmitter (undersupply linked to seizures, tremors, insomnia). Glutamate — major excitatory neurotransmitter, involved in memory. Endorphins — perception of pain/pleasure (oversupply of opioids can suppress natural endorphin production).

## The Nervous System

**Q: What are the two main divisions of the nervous system?**
A: The central nervous system (CNS) — the brain and spinal cord — and the peripheral nervous system (PNS), which contains all nerves outside the CNS and connects the CNS to the rest of the body.

**Q: What are the three types of neurons and their roles?**
A: Sensory neurons carry incoming messages from the body's tissues and receptors to the spinal cord and brain. Motor neurons carry outgoing instructions from the CNS to the muscles. Interneurons, found in the brain and spinal cord, process information between sensory input and motor output.

**Q: How is the peripheral nervous system organized?**
A: Into the somatic nervous system (controls skeletal muscles) and the autonomic nervous system, or ANS (controls glands and internal organ muscles). The ANS splits further into the sympathetic nervous system (arouses the body — fight-or-flight) and the parasympathetic nervous system (calms the body, conserves energy).

## The Endocrine System

**Q: How does the endocrine system communicate, and how is that different from the nervous system?**
A: It's the body's "slow" chemical communication system — a set of glands and fat tissue that secrete hormones into the bloodstream. Hormone effects last longer than the nervous system's electrochemical signals, but take longer to arrive.

**Q: What do the adrenal glands do?**
A: Sitting above the kidneys, they secrete epinephrine (adrenaline) and norepinephrine, which arouse the body during stress and support the fight-or-flight response.

**Q: What is the role of the pituitary gland?**
A: Often called the master gland, it's controlled by the hypothalamus and releases hormones — including growth hormone and oxytocin — that trigger other endocrine glands to release their own hormones.

## Tools for Studying the Brain

**Q: What do EEG and MEG measure?**
A: EEG (electroencephalogram) uses scalp electrodes to record electrical activity sweeping across the brain's surface. MEG (magnetoencephalography) uses a head coil to measure magnetic fields generated by that same natural electrical activity.

**Q: What's the difference between PET, MRI, and fMRI?**
A: PET (positron emission tomography) tracks a radioactive form of glucose to show which brain areas are active during a task. MRI uses magnetic fields and radio waves to produce detailed images of brain anatomy (structure). fMRI compares successive MRI scans to reveal blood flow and brain function (activity), not just structure.

## Brain Structures

**Q: What structures make up the brainstem, and what do they do?**
A: The brainstem is the brain's oldest, innermost region, responsible for automatic survival functions. The medulla controls heartbeat and breathing. The pons helps coordinate movement and control sleep. The thalamus (sensory control center) directs sensory messages to the cortex. The reticular formation filters information and plays a key role in arousal.

**Q: What does the cerebellum do?**
A: Located at the rear of the brainstem, it coordinates voluntary movement, balance, and aids in processing and storing implicit memories (like riding a bike) — plus judgment of time and emotional control.

**Q: What structures make up the limbic system, and what does each do?**
A: The limbic system sits between the brain's oldest and newest areas and is linked to emotion and drives. The amygdala enables aggression and fear. The hypothalamus (below the thalamus) governs hunger, thirst, body temperature, and links to the endocrine system via the pituitary gland. The hippocampus processes conscious, explicit memories of facts and events.

**Q: What are the four lobes of the cerebral cortex and their primary functions?**
A: Frontal lobes — speech, muscle movement, planning, judgment (includes motor cortex and the prefrontal cortex, tied to forward thinking and self-control). Parietal lobes — touch and body position (includes somatosensory cortex). Occipital lobes — vision (includes visual cortex). Temporal lobes — hearing (includes auditory cortex).

**Q: What are association areas?**
A: Areas of the cerebral cortex, found in all four lobes, involved in higher mental functions like learning, thinking, remembering, and speaking — rather than a single sensory or motor function.

**Q: What happened to Phineas Gage, and why does it matter?**
A: An iron rod driven through his skull in an 1848 railroad accident destroyed much of his frontal lobe. He survived but underwent significant personality changes, offering early evidence connecting the frontal lobes to judgment, planning, and self-control.

**Q: How does the brain respond to damage?**
A: Through neuroplasticity — reorganizing existing tissue and, to a limited degree, neurogenesis (forming new neurons) — though severed neurons in the brain and spinal cord generally don't repair themselves.

**Q: What is the corpus callosum, and what happens in a "split brain"?**
A: The corpus callosum is the band of neural fibers connecting the brain's two hemispheres. Cutting it (as in split-brain surgery) prevents the two hemispheres from sharing information, revealing that the left hemisphere excels at quick, literal language interpretation, while the right hemisphere is stronger at inference and reasoned conclusions.

## Consciousness & Attention

**Q: What is consciousness?**
A: Subjective awareness of the self and environment. It enables voluntary control, communication of mental states, and focused attention — and is a product of coordinated, cortex-wide brain activity.

**Q: What's the difference between sequential and parallel processing?**
A: Sequential processing handles one aspect of a task at a time and is generally needed for new or difficult tasks. Parallel processing handles many aspects of a problem simultaneously and is the brain's natural mode for most functions, including vision.

**Q: What is inattentional blindness?**
A: Failing to notice visible objects or events because attention is directed elsewhere — like viewers tracking a basketball pass missing an umbrella-toting figure walking through the scene.

## Sleep & Dreams

**Q: What is circadian rhythm?**
A: The body's internal 24-hour biological clock, which regulates alertness and sleepiness and can be altered by age and experience.

**Q: What are the stages of a typical sleep cycle?**
A: N1 (light, drifting-off sleep), N2 (deeper, marked by sleep spindles), N3 (deep, slow-wave sleep with delta waves), and REM (rapid eye movement sleep, where vivid dreaming commonly occurs). N1, N2, and N3 together are called NREM sleep.

**Q: Why is REM called "paradoxical sleep"?**
A: Because the body is internally aroused (rapid brain waves, increased heart rate, irregular breathing) but externally calm — the brainstem blocks motor cortex messages so the major muscles stay relaxed.

**Q: What brain waves are associated with each state?**
A: Beta waves — alert, waking state. Alpha waves — awake but relaxed. Delta waves — large, slow waves of deep N3 sleep. REM waves resemble the near-waking waves of N1, despite the body being more internally aroused during REM.

**Q: Why do we sleep?**
A: Sleep protects, aids physical recovery, restores and rebuilds memories, feeds creative thinking, and supports growth.

**Q: What are the major sleep disorders?**
A: Insomnia (persistent difficulty falling or staying asleep), narcolepsy (uncontrollable sleep attacks), sleep apnea (repeated breathing interruptions during sleep), sleepwalking/sleeptalking, and night terrors (high arousal with an appearance of terror, mostly in N3 sleep in children).

**Q: According to Freud, what's the difference between manifest and latent dream content?**
A: Manifest content is the remembered, surface storyline of a dream. Latent content is its underlying, hidden meaning.

## Quick Connects

**Q: How does neuroplasticity connect to both childhood development and adult recovery from brain damage?**
A: Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to reorganize itself by building new neural pathways. It's most dramatic in childhood but continues throughout life, which is why the brain can partially reorganize existing tissue after damage even in adulthood.

**Q: How do the limbic system and the endocrine system work together during a stress response?**
A: The amygdala (limbic system) triggers a fear/threat response, the hypothalamus governs the body's stress reaction and signals the pituitary gland, and the pituitary in turn signals the adrenal glands to release stress hormones like epinephrine — linking brain structures to whole-body physiological arousal.
