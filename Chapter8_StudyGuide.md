## Part 1: Thinking

### Concepts & Problem Solving

**Q: What is cognition, and how does metacognition help students succeed?**
A: Cognition refers to all mental activities associated with thinking, knowing, remembering, and communicating. Metacognition — thinking about our own thinking — helps students succeed academically because monitoring and evaluating your own learning reveals what you don't yet know, allowing you to focus effort where it's needed.

**Q: What is a concept, and what role does a prototype play?**
A: A concept is a mental grouping of similar objects, events, ideas, or people that simplifies thinking. A prototype is the best (most typical) example of a concept. We more quickly recognize something as belonging to a category when it closely matches the prototype — for example, a robin feels more "bird-like" than a penguin.

**Q: What is the difference between an algorithm and a heuristic?**
A: An algorithm is a methodical, step-by-step procedure that guarantees a solution but can take significant time and effort. A heuristic is a mental shortcut that enables quick, efficient judgments but risks error. Example: computing every possible word from 10 letters (algorithm) vs. grouping likely letter combinations first (heuristic).

**Q: What happens in the brain during an insight (Aha!) moment?**
A: A burst of activity appears in the right temporal lobe just above the ear. Before the moment, the frontal lobes are active (focusing attention); at the instant of insight, the right temporal lobe fires — consistent with sudden creative connections being made.

**Q: What is confirmation bias, and how did Wason's 2-4-6 study demonstrate it?**
A: Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for information that supports our existing beliefs while ignoring contradictory evidence. In Wason's study, most participants formed a hypothesis ("counting by twos") and then tested only examples that confirmed it (6-8-10), never testing sequences that could disprove it. They were seldom right but rarely doubted themselves.

**Q: What is fixation, and why is it an obstacle to problem solving?**
A: Fixation is an inability to view a problem from a new angle — being mentally "stuck" on one approach. The matchstick problem illustrates this: most people fixate on 2D solutions when the answer requires thinking in three dimensions.

### Decisions and Judgments

**Q: What is intuition, and when does it help or hurt us?**
A: Intuition is fast, automatic, unreasoned thought based on accumulated experience. It aids survival and often serves us well (e.g., experienced nurses quickly spotting a deteriorating patient). It can hurt us when it leads us to "overfeel and underthink," especially with complex probability problems.

**Q: How does the representativeness heuristic lead to errors?**
A: We judge the likelihood of something by how closely it matches a prototype, often ignoring base rates. Example: A short, slim poetry reader seems more likely to be an Ivy League English professor than a truck driver — but with ~400 professors and 3.5 million truck drivers, the base rate makes "truck driver" far more probable.

**Q: How does the availability heuristic distort risk assessment?**
A: We estimate how common an event is based on how easily examples come to mind. Vivid, recent, or emotionally charged events (plane crashes, terrorist attacks) feel more frequent than they statistically are, while mundane but more common risks (car accidents, heart disease) feel less threatening.

**Q: What is overconfidence, and what is the planning fallacy?**
A: Overconfidence is overestimating the accuracy of our beliefs and judgments. The planning fallacy is a related tendency to overestimate how quickly we will finish tasks. Research shows that people in general rate themselves as more confident than correct, and student projects typically take about twice as long as predicted.

**Q: What is belief perseverance, and what is the most effective remedy?**
A: Belief perseverance is clinging to existing beliefs even when evidence disproves them. In the death penalty study, showing both sides the same mixed evidence actually increased their disagreement. The most effective remedy is to actively consider the opposite — imagine what you would think if the results had come out the other way.

**Q: How does framing affect decision making? Give an example.**
A: The way information is presented can strongly influence choices. Tversky and Kahneman showed that people strongly prefer a policy framed as saving 200 lives over an identical policy framed as letting 400 people die, even though the outcomes are mathematically equivalent. Defaults also matter: opt-out organ donation systems achieve nearly 100% participation; opt-in systems achieve far less.

### Creativity

**Q: How does divergent thinking differ from convergent thinking?**
A: Convergent thinking finds the single correct answer — the kind measured by aptitude tests. Divergent thinking generates many novel options (e.g., listing every use for a brick) — the kind measured by creativity tests.

**Q: What are Sternberg's five ingredients of creativity?**
A: (1) Expertise — a solid knowledge base; (2) Imaginative thinking skills — seeing patterns and making new connections; (3) A venturesome personality — risk-taking and tolerance for ambiguity; (4) Intrinsic motivation — driven by pleasure of the work, not external rewards; (5) A creative environment — colleagues and settings that spark and refine ideas.

## Part 2: Language

**Q: What does Chomsky mean by universal grammar?**
A: Chomsky proposed that humans are born with a built-in readiness (predisposition) to learn grammatical rules. This explains why children in all cultures acquire language naturally, quickly, and with little formal teaching — language acquisition unfolds somewhat like birds learning to fly.

**Q: What is the sequence of language development in children?**
A: (1) ~4 months: babbling — spontaneous sounds from all languages; (2) ~10 months: babbling resembles the household language; (3) ~12 months: one-word stage — single words carry whole meanings; (4) ~24 months: two-word telegraphic speech; (5) 24+ months: rapid development into complex sentences.

**Q: What is the difference between receptive and productive language?**
A: Receptive language is the ability to understand what is said; it develops first. Productive language is the ability to produce words; it follows receptive language.

**Q: What is the critical period for language, and what happens if it is missed?**
A: Childhood appears to be a sensitive (critical) period during which certain aspects of language are most easily mastered. Children not exposed to any spoken or signed language until age 7 or beyond lose the ability to fully acquire any language. Second-language learners who start after this window almost always retain an accent and imperfect grammar.

**Q: What are Broca's area and Wernicke's area, and what happens when each is damaged?**
A: Broca's area (left frontal lobe): damage causes difficulty producing speech while comprehension stays relatively intact. Wernicke's area (left temporal lobe): damage causes fluent but meaningless speech and an inability to understand others.

**Q: Can non-human animals use language?**
A: Some animals display basic language-like communication (alarm calls, symbol sequences). Chimpanzees and bonobos have learned sign language or symbol systems and can combine them meaningfully. However, apes' vocabularies and sentence structures are far simpler than a 2-year-old child's, and critics note that some apparent "language" may be conditioned responding. Most psychologists agree that if language requires complex grammar and idea exchange, humans alone truly possess it.

## Part 3: Intelligence

### What Is Intelligence?

**Q: How does psychology define intelligence?**
A: Intelligence is the ability to learn from experience, solve problems, and adapt to new situations using knowledge. The definition varies by cultural context — what counts as intelligent varies across settings.

**Q: What is Spearman's g factor, and what evidence supports it?**
A: Spearman used factor analysis to show that people who score high in one mental ability (e.g., verbal) also tend to score higher in other areas (e.g., spatial, mathematical). He argued this reflects a single underlying general intelligence (g). Brain research supports g: a coordinating network enables our many separate neural networks to work together.

**Q: How do fluid and crystallized intelligence differ, and how do they change with age?**
A: Fluid intelligence (Gf) is the ability to reason speedily and abstractly with novel problems — it peaks in the late 20s to early 30s and declines with age. Crystallized intelligence (Gc) is accumulated knowledge reflected in vocabulary and expertise — it continues to grow into old age.

**Q: What are Gardner's eight (possibly nine) intelligences?**
A: Linguistic, logical-mathematical, musical, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalistic — and possibly existential. Gardner's evidence includes people with savant syndrome (exceptional skill in one domain despite low general ability) and cases where brain damage destroys one ability while leaving others intact.

**Q: What are Sternberg's three intelligences, and how do they differ from Gardner's?**
A: Analytical (academic problem-solving), creative (novel situations), and practical (everyday problem management). Unlike Gardner's eight, Sternberg's three are more reliably measured and map more directly to real-world success outcomes.

**Q: What are the four components of emotional intelligence?**
A: Perceiving emotions (in faces, music, stories, and oneself), understanding emotions (predicting how they change and blend), managing emotions (expressing them appropriately, handling others'), and using emotions to facilitate adaptive or creative thinking. Emotionally intelligent people tend to succeed socially and professionally where academically smarter but emotionally less aware people may fail.

### Assessing Intelligence

**Q: What is the difference between an achievement test and an aptitude test?**
A: An achievement test reflects what you have already learned (e.g., a final exam). An aptitude test predicts what you will be able to learn (e.g., the SAT).

**Q: How was the IQ originally calculated, and how is it calculated today?**
A: Originally: (mental age ÷ chronological age) × 100. Today, IQ scores are assigned relative to same-age peers — the average is set at 100, and about 68% of people score between 85 and 115. The formula was abandoned because it broke down for adults.

**Q: What are the three requirements for a "good" intelligence test?**
A: (1) Standardization — tested on a representative sample so your score is comparable to others'; (2) Reliability — gives consistent results when retaken; (3) Validity — measures or predicts what it is supposed to (predictive validity).

### Nature and Nurture of Intelligence

**Q: What does heritability mean, and what does it NOT mean?**
A: Heritability is the proportion of variation among individuals in a group that is attributable to genetic differences. It does NOT mean that 50–80% of any one person's intelligence is genetic — heritability describes group variation, not individual traits.

**Q: What do twin and adoption studies show about genes and intelligence?**
A: Identical twins raised apart have nearly identical IQ scores, showing strong genetic influence. Adopted children's scores ultimately correlate more with biological parents than with adoptive parents. However, adoption from poverty into financially secure homes also raises IQ scores by several points — showing environment matters too.

**Q: What is a growth mindset, and why does it matter academically?**
A: A growth mindset is the belief that intelligence is changeable and grows through effort. Dweck's research shows it makes students more resilient when facing difficult material and more likely to persist. Even brief interventions fostering a growth mindset have modestly improved grades in studies of tens of thousands of students.

**Q: What is stereotype threat, and how can it affect test performance?**
A: Stereotype threat is the self-fulfilling concern that you will be judged negatively based on a stereotype about your group. It can hijack working memory and impair performance on tests. Research shows Black students scored lower when reminded of their race before verbal aptitude tests; women scored lower on math tests when told that women typically underperform on it.
