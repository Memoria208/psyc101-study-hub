# Chapter 7 Study Guide: Memory
*Myers & DeWall (2023) — Psychology in Everyday Life, 6th ed.*

## Part 1: Studying Memory

**Q: What is memory, and what does the information-processing model propose?**
A: Memory is learning that persists over time — our ability to encode, store, and retrieve information. The information-processing model compares memory to a computer: to remember something, we must encode (get information in), store (retain it), and retrieve (get it back out).

**Q: What is the Atkinson-Shiffrin three-stage model, and how has it been updated?**
A: Atkinson and Shiffrin proposed that information first enters sensory memory, moves to short-term memory (where it's encoded through rehearsal), and finally into long-term memory. Later research added two key updates: the short-term stage is now better understood as working memory (an active processing "scratchpad" that links new input with existing long-term memories), and researchers recognize that some information reaches long-term memory automatically, without conscious attention.

**Q: What is working memory, and why does focused attention matter for it?**
A: Working memory is the system that holds information in mind while actively processing it — linking new experience with long-term memory. Without focused attention, information fades quickly. In one study, people who believed information would be available online later invested less effort in remembering it and recalled it worse — the "online, out of mind" effect.

## Part 2: Building Memories — Encoding

**Q: What is the difference between implicit and explicit memory, and how are they processed?**
A: Implicit (nondeclarative) memories — automatic skills and classically conditioned associations — form through automatic processing, bypassing conscious effort. Explicit (declarative) memories — facts and experiences we can consciously "declare" — are encoded through effortful, conscious processing. This reflects the mind's two-track system.

**Q: What kinds of information do we process automatically?**
A: Space (where information appeared on a page), time (the sequence of the day's events), and frequency (how often something has happened) are all typically encoded automatically, without deliberate effort.

**Q: How does sensory memory work, and what are iconic and echoic memory?**
A: Sensory memory is the very first, fleeting stage of memory. Iconic memory is a near-instant, picture-image memory of visual stimuli lasting a few tenths of a second. Echoic memory is a lingering memory of sounds lasting 3–4 seconds. Sperling's letter-grid experiment showed people could briefly hold all nine letters in sensory memory, even though only the cued row could be reported.

**Q: What is the capacity and duration of short-term memory?**
A: George Miller's "Magical Number Seven" suggested we can hold about 7 (± 2) bits of information briefly, though the number varies by task (around 6 letters, 5 words). Without active rehearsal, short-term memories decay quickly — in the Peterson & Peterson study, participants distracted from rehearsing forgot three-consonant strings almost entirely within about 12 seconds.

**Q: What are chunking and mnemonics, and why do they help?**
A: Chunking organizes information into familiar, meaningful units (e.g., remembering a sentence rather than 43 individual letters), making it far easier to encode and recall. Mnemonics are memory aids that typically rely on vivid imagery; concrete, image-rich words and ideas are recalled better than abstract ones.

**Q: Why does cramming fail compared to distributed practice, and what is the testing effect?**
A: The spacing effect shows that distributed (spaced) practice over time produces much better long-term retention than massed practice (cramming), which produces fast but shallow, quickly-forgotten learning. The testing effect shows that actively retrieving information (self-testing) strengthens memory far more than simply rereading it — practicing retrieval, not passive review, is what builds lasting memory.

**Q: Why does making information meaningful improve encoding?**
A: Information that is personally relevant, related to existing knowledge, or organized into a clear structure is far easier to encode and retrieve than isolated, arbitrary material. In one classic study, a vague paragraph about "the procedure" was barely remembered until people were told it described doing laundry — meaningful context made it memorable.

## Part 3: Storage — Retaining Information in the Brain

**Q: Do we run out of storage space for long-term memories?**
A: No — unlike Sherlock Holmes's "little empty attic" metaphor, the brain's storage capacity for long-term memories has no practical limit; researchers have compared its scale to the World Wide Web.

**Q: What roles do the hippocampus and frontal lobes play in explicit memory?**
A: The hippocampus is the brain's "save button" for new explicit memories — it registers and temporarily stores the elements of an event (facts, episodes) before they migrate to the cortex for permanent storage, a process called memory consolidation. Sleep strongly supports this consolidation process. The left and right frontal lobes handle different types of memory (e.g., left for recalling a password, right for visual imagery).

**Q: What roles do the cerebellum and basal ganglia play in implicit memory?**
A: The cerebellum is essential for forming and storing memories created through classical conditioning (e.g., a conditioned eye blink). The basal ganglia help form implicit memories for physical skills and procedures, such as riding a bike. Patients who lose their hippocampus can still form these implicit memories, showing implicit and explicit memory rely on separate brain systems.

**Q: Why do we experience infantile amnesia?**
A: Before about age 3–4, we lack a fully developed hippocampus and the language skills needed to encode and store explicit memories, so as adults we cannot consciously recall our earliest years — even though implicit learning (like early motor skills) from that period can still influence us.

**Q: How does the amygdala contribute to memory, and what are flashbulb memories?**
A: Stress and excitement trigger stress hormones that provoke the amygdala to boost activity in memory-forming brain areas, "searing" arousing events into memory more strongly than mundane ones. Flashbulb memories are vivid, detailed mental "snapshots" of exciting or shocking events (e.g., where you were during a major news event), though they can still contain errors that creep in over time.

**Q: What happens at the synapse level during learning, and what is long-term potentiation (LTP)?**
A: Learning strengthens synaptic connections — sending neurons release neurotransmitters more efficiently, and receiving neurons may grow more receptor sites. This increased efficiency, called long-term potentiation (LTP), is considered the physical basis of learning and memory; drugs that block LTP interfere with learning, while drugs that enhance it improve it.

## Part 4: Retrieval — Getting Information Out

**Q: What are the three main ways psychologists measure memory retention?**
A: Recall (retrieving information with no cues, as in a fill-in-the-blank question), recognition (identifying previously learned material, as in multiple choice), and relearning (learning something faster the second time). People often recognize far more than they can recall — one study found 25-years-later graduates could recognize 90% of old classmates' photos despite being able to recall few names unprompted.

**Q: How do retrieval cues and priming work?**
A: Retrieval cues are like anchor points in a web of associations built at encoding — the more cues encoded (smells, moods, surroundings), the more paths there are back to a memory. Priming is the unconscious activation of associations (e.g., hearing "rabbit" makes "hare" more available) that can influence perception and behavior without our awareness.

**Q: What are context-dependent and state-dependent memory?**
A: Context-dependent memory means retrieval improves when we're back in the same physical setting where we encoded the information (e.g., scuba divers recalled words better in the same environment — underwater or on the beach — where they learned them). State-dependent memory is the parallel effect for internal physiological/emotional states (e.g., information learned while intoxicated is recalled slightly better while intoxicated again).

**Q: What is mood-congruent memory?**
A: The tendency to recall memories and interpret situations in ways that match our current mood — happy moods prime pleasant memories and generous interpretations of others, while sad or angry moods prime unhappy memories, which can help maintain and prolong that mood.

**Q: What is the serial position effect?**
A: The tendency to recall the first items in a list best (primacy effect, from more rehearsal) and the last items best immediately after (recency effect, because they're still in working memory) — leaving the middle items most likely to be forgotten.

## Part 5: Forgetting

**Q: Is forgetting always bad?**
A: No — forgetting outdated or irrelevant information (old phone numbers, yesterday's outfit) is adaptive and helps us focus on what matters most. People with extraordinarily rare "highly superior autobiographical memory," who cannot forget, often find that flood of memory intrusive and difficult to manage.

**Q: What are the three points at which forgetting can occur?**
A: Encoding failure (information was never attended to or encoded in the first place), storage decay (a memory trace physically fades over time, as shown in Ebbinghaus's forgetting curve — rapid loss at first, then leveling off), and retrieval failure (information was stored but cannot currently be accessed, as in tip-of-the-tongue moments).

**Q: What is the difference between proactive and retroactive interference?**
A: Proactive interference is when older learning disrupts recall of new information (an old lock combination interfering with a new one). Retroactive interference is when new learning disrupts recall of older information (new lyrics to a familiar tune making the original hard to recall). Learning material right before sleep suffers less retroactive interference, because there's less new information competing for storage afterward.

**Q: What is positive transfer?**
A: The facilitating effect that previously learned material can have on new learning — for example, already knowing Latin can make learning French easier, rather than interfering with it.

**Q: What is repression, and what does current research say about it?**
A: Repression is Freud's proposed defense mechanism in which the mind banishes painful or unacceptable memories from conscious awareness to reduce anxiety. While still a popular belief, memory researchers now think true repression rarely, if ever, occurs — trauma more often produces vivid, intrusive memories (via stress hormones sharpening attention) rather than forgotten ones, as shown in studies of plane-crash survivors and other trauma survivors.

**Q: What is the difference between anterograde and retrograde amnesia?**
A: Anterograde amnesia is the inability to form new conscious (explicit) memories after a brain injury, while past memories remain intact (as with patient Henry Molaison). Retrograde amnesia is the inability to retrieve memories from before the injury. Patients with anterograde amnesia can still form new implicit memories (skills, conditioning) even though they have no conscious awareness of having learned them.

## Part 6: Memory Construction Errors

**Q: In what sense is all memory "constructed," and what is reconsolidation?**
A: We don't simply retrieve memories intact — we reweave them each time, blending stored information with later imagination, expectation, and new input. Reconsolidation is the process by which a retrieved memory can be subtly altered before being "re-stored," meaning frequently replayed memories are more likely to have changed over time.

**Q: What is the misinformation effect, and what did Loftus and Palmer's experiment show?**
A: The misinformation effect is confidently misremembering an event after being exposed to subtly misleading information about it after the fact. In Loftus and Palmer's classic study, people asked how fast cars were going when they "smashed into" (vs. "hit") each other gave higher speed estimates and were more likely to falsely recall broken glass that was never in the film.

**Q: What is imagination inflation?**
A: Repeatedly imagining an event that never happened increases confidence that it really did happen, sometimes producing detailed false memories — even of committing a crime, in controlled experiments.

**Q: What is source amnesia, and how does it relate to déjà vu?**
A: Source amnesia is retaining a memory of an event or piece of information while forgetting where, when, or from whom it was learned (e.g., misremembering who told you a piece of gossip). It's a major contributor to false memories and helps explain déjà vu — a fleeting, eerie sense of familiarity with a new situation, which may arise when we feel familiarity (via the temporal lobe) without consciously recalling the specific prior context (via the hippocampus).

**Q: Why are false memories so convincing, and what practical lesson follows?**
A: Because the misinformation effect and source amnesia both operate outside conscious awareness, false memories can feel just as real and vivid as true ones — people often falsely "recognize" words or details that fit the general gist of what they experienced, even when those specific details never occurred. The practical takeaway: memory is reconstructive, not a perfect recording, so don't assume vividness or confidence guarantees accuracy — your own or an eyewitness's.
